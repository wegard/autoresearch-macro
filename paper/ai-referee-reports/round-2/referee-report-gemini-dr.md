# Referee Report for the International Journal of Forecasting: "Can Agentic Search Improve Foundation Model Forecasts for Small Open Economies?"

### Summary

The manuscript under consideration investigates a highly contemporary intersection of automated machine learning (AutoML), time series foundation models (TSFMs), and macroeconomic forecasting within the structural context of a small open economy. Specifically, the study evaluates whether an agentic search framework—orchestrated by a large language model (LLM), Claude Sonnet—can iteratively optimize a forecasting pipeline built upon the 120-million parameter Chronos-2 foundation model.[1] The empirical application targets the Norwegian macroeconomy, generating multi-step point forecasts (1, 3, 6, and 12 months ahead) for consumer price index (CPI) inflation, industrial production, retail sales, and the unemployment rate.[1] To construct the optimization environment, the authors adapt the autoresearch framework introduced by Karpathy (2026), granting the LLM agent the autonomy to navigate a constrained configuration space that includes the selection of macroeconomic covariates, data transformations, context window lengths, and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) fine-tuning hyperparameters.[1, 2] The evaluation protocol partitions the data into a validation era (2006–2015) for agentic pipeline discovery and a held-out test era (2016–2025) for out-of-sample performance verification.[1]

The findings presented in the manuscript reveal a profound and instructive dichotomy that carries significant implications for the field of macroeconomic forecasting. During the in-sample validation period, the agentic search successfully navigates the high-dimensional configuration space to discover a highly interpretable and economically coherent pipeline. By selecting oil prices, the Norges Bank policy rate, United States inflation, and the NOK/EUR exchange rate, alongside a truncated 96-month context window, the automated agent reduces the mean absolute scaled error (MASE) by 6.8% relative to a purely zero-shot, univariate baseline.[1] However, when this optimized pipeline is deployed in the out-of-sample test period—an era characterized by severe structural breaks, including the COVID-19 pandemic and the abrupt, post-2022 global inflation surge—the agent-tuned configuration demonstrates catastrophic overfitting.[1] A meticulous step-by-step ablation analysis exposes a "scissors effect," wherein every incremental pipeline modification that enhanced validation-era performance monotonically degraded the model's capacity to generalize to the test era.[1] The incorporation of the monetary policy rate covariate is identified as the single largest contributor to this out-of-sample deterioration, reflecting the violent regime shift from the zero-lower-bound environment to the rapid tightening cycle of 2022–2024.[1]

Conversely, the purely zero-shot application of the Chronos-2 foundation model exhibits extraordinary robustness in the face of these profound regime shifts. Stripped of task-specific covariate engineering and local fine-tuning, the zero-shot configuration systematically outperforms not only the agent-tuned pipeline but also a comprehensive suite of classical econometric baselines, including Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models, Vector Autoregressions (VAR), and dynamic factor models.[1] This outcome suggests that the massive, heterogeneous pre-training corpora utilized by modern TSFMs impart an implicit regularization that guards against the specific forms of covariate overfitting that plague traditional, fixed-window macroeconomic optimization.[1, 3] The identification and empirical documentation of this "overfitting paradox" within an LLM-guided agentic search represent a novel and highly relevant contribution to the literature.[1, 4] Nevertheless, to achieve the rigorous publication standards expected by the *International Journal of Forecasting*, the manuscript requires substantial methodological refinement. The subsequent sections of this report detail critical areas demanding expansion, encompassing the theoretical integrity of the data vintages utilized, the architectural breadth of the foundation model baselines, the imperative for probabilistic forecast evaluation, and the necessity of dynamic adaptation within the agentic search algorithm itself.

### Major comments

**1. The Real-Time Data Fallacy in Automated Macroeconomic Search Environments**

A foundational requirement for any rigorous macroeconomic forecast evaluation is the absolute fidelity of the information set available to the predictive algorithm at any given historical forecast origin. The manuscript currently relies upon a "pseudo-real-time" protocol, which the authors define as the utilization of latest-vintage data systematically obscured by chronological publication lags to simulate the "ragged edge" characteristic of real-world macroeconomic data releases.[1] While adjusting for asynchronous publication delays constitutes a necessary minimum standard, the reliance on fully revised, end-of-sample (EOS) data introduces a profound look-ahead bias that critically undermines the evaluation of the entire agentic search procedure.[5, 6, 7]

Macroeconomic indicators are not static entities; variables such as industrial production and retail sales undergo substantial, multi-year, and often non-random revisions as statistical agencies gradually aggregate comprehensive census data to replace preliminary sample estimates.[5, 7] The extensive literature on real-time macroeconomic forecasting has conclusively demonstrated that models, optimization routines, and covariate selections optimized on fully revised EOS data frequently fail catastrophically when deployed upon the noisy, unrevised real-time-vintage (RTV) data that actual practitioners must consume in real time.[6, 7, 8] 

In the specific context of the manuscript's LLM-guided agentic search, this distinction transcends a mere technicality and strikes at the core validity of the optimization mechanism. The Claude Sonnet agent iteratively selects covariates, tests transformations, and accepts or rejects configurations based on rolling validation performance spanning 2006 to 2015.[1] If the agent evaluates the predictive utility of an industrial production lag using fully revised 2025 data to simulate a 2010 forecast origin, the agent is effectively discovering statistical relationships and structural smoothings that literally did not exist in the information set available to any forecaster in 2010.[6, 8] Time series foundation models, highly sensitive to high-frequency noise and volatility, are particularly susceptible to this bias.[9, 10] The agent might accurately reject a specific covariate based on the high variance of its real-time preliminary release, whereas it erroneously accepts the smoothed, finalized EOS version, leading to the reported 6.8% validation improvement [1]—an improvement that is likely an artifact of data revision rather than a genuine extraction of predictive signal.

To align with the established standards of the *International Journal of Forecasting*, the empirical analysis must fundamentally reconstruct the simulation environment using true real-time data vintages for any series subject to statistical revision.

| Feature Comparison | Pseudo-Real-Time (Latest-Vintage) | True Real-Time (RTV) | Implications for Agentic Search |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Data Source** | Final, fully revised dataset. | Origin-specific vintage datasets. | LLM evaluates historical performance based on data that was actually published at time *t*. |
| **Noise Profile** | Artificially smooth; preliminary estimation errors are erased. | Highly noisy; contains preliminary estimation errors and subsequent benchmark revisions. | Agents optimizing on latest-vintage data will consistently over-select covariates that appear historically smooth but are practically unusable in real-world deployment. |
| **Look-ahead Bias** | High. Future information is embedded in historical revisions. | Zero. Strict adherence to historical information sets. | Ensures the validation-era MASE improvements are theoretically sound and practically attainable by forecasting institutions. |

The Norges Bank maintains robust real-time database archives, and equivalent historical matrices can be constructed from Statistics Norway.[1, 11] At a minimum, the manuscript requires a comprehensive sensitivity analysis that re-runs the 50-iteration agentic search utilizing RTV data for a representative subset of the target variables to ascertain whether the agent discovers an entirely divergent covariate pipeline when confronted with true real-time statistical noise.

**2. Foundation Model Monoculture and Architectural Confounding**

The manuscript predicates its entire analysis of foundation model forecasting upon a single architecture: the 120-million parameter Chronos-2 model.[1] While Chronos-2 represents a state-of-the-art approach, its specific architecture—treating continuous time series forecasting as a discrete language modeling problem via value quantization, tokenization, and an encoder-decoder sequence generation mechanism—represents only one theoretical paradigm within the rapidly fragmenting TSFM ecosystem.[12, 13, 14] Furthermore, the manner in which Chronos-2 handles multivariate inputs relies on a highly specific group attention mechanism that alternates between time attention (within a series) and group attention (across related series in a batch).[12, 13, 14] 

The literature has firmly established that the fundamental architectural choices of a TSFM dictate its interaction with exogenous covariates and its resilience to out-of-distribution shocks.[10] For instance, Google's TimesFM 2.5 utilizes a decoder-only architecture combined with patch-level tokenization, treating contiguous sequences of time points as single tokens rather than quantizing individual values.[10, 15] Conversely, the Moirai-2 model, developed by Salesforce and trained on the massive 27-billion observation LOTSA corpus, employs an any-variate masked encoder mechanism that processes multivariate dynamics fundamentally differently than Chronos-2.[10, 16] Additionally, domain-specific oracles such as the BISTRO model, recently introduced by the Bank for International Settlements (BIS), are fine-tuned explicitly on exhaustive repositories of macroeconomic aggregates, resulting in highly specialized structural priors that may navigate regime shifts with far greater agility than general-purpose models.[17, 18, 19]

| Model Framework | Architectural Paradigm | Multivariate Processing | Economic Domain Specificity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chronos-2** | Encoder-Decoder, Value Quantization | Group Attention across parallel tokens | General Purpose (Synthetic + Real) |
| **TimesFM 2.5** | Decoder-only, Patch Tokenization | Co-embedded patches | General Purpose (Massive Real) |
| **Moirai-2** | Any-variate Masked Encoder | Multivariate attention matrices | General Purpose (LOTSA Corpus) |
| **BISTRO (BIS)** | Transformer-based sequence oracle | Macro-specific conditioning | High (Fine-tuned strictly on global macroeconomic data) |

The manuscript's central theoretical claim—that zero-shot robustness dominates agent-tuned configurations during structural breaks, leading to the "overfitting paradox"—is currently profoundly entangled with the specific tokenization schemes and group attention artifacts of Chronos-2.[1] To elevate this finding from an algorithmic curiosity to a generalized econometric principle, the experimental design must integrate at least one fundamentally divergent TSFM baseline (e.g., TimesFM 2.5 or Moirai-2).[10, 20] Evaluating whether the "scissors effect" persists across disparate architectures will conclusively determine if the failure of the agentic pipeline is an intrinsic property of automated macroeconomic feature engineering, or merely an artifact of the interaction between LoRA fine-tuning and the Chronos-2 attention module.[1, 13]

**3. The Critical Absence of Probabilistic and Density Forecast Evaluation**

The *International Journal of Forecasting* maintains a rigorous emphasis on the evaluation of forecast uncertainty, operating under the established consensus that deterministic point forecasts are fundamentally insufficient for comprehensive macroeconomic risk assessment and policy formulation.[21, 22] The foundation model explicitly selected by the authors, Chronos-2, is inherently designed to produce probabilistic outputs; it natively generates 21 distinct predictive quantiles ranging from the 1st to the 99th percentile, facilitating the construction of highly granular predictive distributions.[13, 14] Despite leveraging a model architecturally optimized for distributional prediction, the manuscript restricts its evaluation entirely to point forecast accuracy, relying exclusively on metrics such as Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE).[1]

This procedural limitation severely obscures the analytical depth of the agentic search's impact on the model's behavior.[3, 23] The agent-tuned configuration is heavily penalized in the point-forecast evaluation during the post-COVID regime shift (2022 onwards) because the median point estimates fail to capture the unprecedented magnitude of the global inflation surge.[1] However, this point-estimate failure may mask critical distributional information.[18] It is highly plausible that the LLM-optimized model, while yielding higher absolute errors at the median, produced significantly wider, highly asymmetric, and more accurately calibrated predictive distributions during periods of elevated macroeconomic volatility. If the agent-tuned model successfully recognized the breakdown in the policy rate relationship and responded by dramatically increasing the variance of its predictive quantiles, it effectively captured the heightened risk of the regime shift—an accomplishment that point metrics inherently ignore but that is paramount for central banks assessing tail risks.[18, 23]

The evaluation of the agentic pipeline must be exhaustively expanded to encompass the full probability density. The Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) and specific Quantile Loss functions must be adopted as co-primary evaluation criteria.[22, 24] By implementing CRPS, the analysis will definitively reveal whether the Claude Sonnet agent's optimization routines improved the model's uncertainty quantification and probabilistic calibration, or if the overfitting paradox systematically degraded the reliability of the entire predictive distribution during the structural breaks of the test era.[3]

**4. Static Agentic Search Within Non-Stationary Macroeconomic Environments**

The manuscript provides a highly compelling and visually stark ablation analysis, documenting how the covariates laboriously discovered during the stable validation era (2006–2015) actively and systematically degraded forecasting performance during the volatile out-of-sample test era (2016–2025).[1] The analysis astutely identifies the macroeconomic regime shift in the monetary policy rate—transitioning from a prolonged zero-lower-bound environment to a historically rapid tightening cycle—as the primary driver of this algorithmic deterioration.[1] However, the experimental design of the agentic search algorithm itself is artificially static: the LLM is authorized to execute its configuration search *only once* over the fixed validation window, after which the resulting forecasting pipeline is immutably frozen for an entire decade of out-of-sample evaluation.[1]

While freezing parameters is standard econometric practice for ensuring strict out-of-sample validity in traditional model evaluation, it fundamentally misrepresents and artificially cripples the operational potential of modern agentic workflows.[2, 4, 25] The Karpathy (2026) autoresearch framework, upon which the study's methodology is based, was explicitly designed to operate as a continuous, perpetual loop of proposal, execution, and evaluation.[2, 26] In a genuine institutional forecasting environment, no automated pipeline would remain unexamined and frozen for ten years while experiencing successive, massive predictive failures.[6, 27] The true theoretical test of an autonomous AI agent operating within a non-stationary economic environment is its capacity for dynamic reflection—its ability to monitor deteriorating test-era performance, discard failed covariates, and discover new, regime-appropriate statistical relationships as the underlying data-generating process evolves.[25, 28]

The experimental design should be fundamentally augmented to incorporate a "rolling search" or "expanding search" variant.[29, 30] For instance, the autoresearch loop could be systematically re-triggered on an annual basis or dynamically initiated at the statistical onset of detected structural breaks. If the LLM agent is granted the operational capacity to iteratively re-evaluate the forecasting pipeline at the conclusion of 2021, does its semantic reasoning capabilities allow it to autonomously recognize that the fundamental macroeconomic relationships governing the policy rate and global oil prices have irreversibly altered?[1, 11, 31] Does the agent subsequently drop the policy rate covariate or radically alter its mathematical transformation? Evaluating the dynamic adaptability of the agentic framework is absolutely essential to determine whether automated pipeline optimization is fundamentally, theoretically flawed for macroeconomic applications, or merely superficially flawed when improperly constrained by static, decade-old validation windows.[1, 28, 32]

**5. The Insufficiency of the Optimization Baselines**

To isolate and quantify the explicit utility of utilizing a Large Language Model (Claude Sonnet) as the central optimization engine, the manuscript contrasts the LLM-guided agentic search trajectory against a naive "random search" baseline.[1] This comparative framework sets an unacceptably low benchmark. Within the advanced domain of automated machine learning (AutoML), random search serves only as a trivial baseline.[33] Modern, state-of-the-art AutoML frameworks rely heavily on highly sophisticated mathematical optimization architectures, most notably Bayesian optimization utilizing Gaussian Processes, and advanced multi-armed bandit approaches such as Successive Halving and Hyperband algorithms.[1, 33] 

The authors explicitly note their utilization of the AutoGluon-TimeSeries interface to operationally access the Chronos-2 model.[1, 34] Given that the AutoGluon framework natively integrates exceptionally powerful hyperparameter optimization routines via Bayesian methods, the manuscript must rigorously benchmark the Claude-driven agentic search against a standard, mathematically optimized Bayesian loop operating over the exact same configuration search space (i.e., covariate selection, data transformations, context length, and LoRA parameters).[1] 

The critical scientific question is not whether a billion-parameter LLM can outperform mathematically uninformed random chance, but whether the LLM's semantic "understanding" of macroeconomic structural relationships—for example, possessing the encoded knowledge that Norway is a small open economy heavily dependent on petroleum exports, and therefore logically prioritizing Brent crude as a covariate [1, 31, 35]—actually yields a more robust, generalizable, or efficient optimization trajectory than gradient-free mathematical approaches like Tree-structured Parzen Estimators (TPE). If a standard, mathematically pure Bayesian optimizer discovers a distinct covariate pipeline that suffers less from the out-of-sample overfitting paradox, it would strongly suggest that the LLM's adherence to traditional macroeconomic "common sense" is actually detrimental and actively misleading in non-stationary, shock-prone environments.

**6. Econometric Interpretation of the Multivariate Baseline Underperformance**

The empirical study reports that classic multivariate econometric methods—specifically Vector Autoregression (VAR) and Principal Component Factor Models—strictly underperform the much simpler univariate baselines (ARIMA, Exponential Smoothing) across both the validation and test eras.[1] The manuscript addresses this phenomenon somewhat superficially, attributing it entirely to the well-documented historical difficulty of beating univariate benchmarks in macroeconomic forecasting.[1] However, this empirical finding warrants significantly deeper econometric interrogation, particularly concerning the structural dynamics of the Norwegian economy and the fundamental nature of the covariates selected.[11, 35]

The explicit failure of the VAR model—despite being generously granted access to the exact same four optimally selected covariates discovered by the agent (oil prices, policy rate, US CPI, exchange rate)—suggests that the underlying relationships between these specific variables and the target macroeconomic aggregates are highly non-linear or subject to severe time-varying parameters (TVP).[6] Traditional econometric VARs operating with fixed coefficients struggle immensely to parse the rapid volatility clustering and sudden parameter drift observed during the unprecedented shocks of the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2022 energy market dislocations.[11, 36] 

The analytical discussion must profoundly expand upon the theoretical mechanisms of this failure. Why does the Chronos-2 zero-shot model succeed where the VAR catastrophically fails? Does the massive pre-training corpus of the foundation model—which includes highly diverse synthetic data generated via Gaussian processes designed to mimic myriad multivariate structures [12, 13, 14]—act as a highly effective, dynamic regularization mechanism, functionally analogous to advanced Bayesian shrinkage techniques (e.g., Minnesota priors or dynamic model averaging)? The discussion section must explicitly bridge the empirical performance of the TSFM to the rich theoretical literature on macroeconomic forecasting under parameter instability, rigorously exploring whether transformer-based foundation models implicitly encode time-varying parameter dynamics through their adaptive attention mechanisms.[7, 17]

### Minor comments

1.  **Diebold-Mariano Test Specifications in Finite Samples:** The manuscript's application of the Diebold and Mariano (2002) test utilizes standard Newey-West HAC standard errors with a bandwidth set to $h-1$.[1] While theoretically standard asymptotically, the extremely small sample size of the specific subperiod analyses (for instance, the COVID-19 subperiod comprises a mere 24 monthly forecast origins) renders asymptotic distributions highly prone to severe size distortions, particularly at the 12-month forecast horizon where temporal overlap is massive.[1] The econometric analysis should implement robust small-sample corrections (e.g., the Harvey-Leybourne-Newbold correction) or rely entirely on finite-sample robust predictive ability tests to ensure the validity of statistical significance claims during the short regime-shift windows.
2.  **MASE Denominator and Intrinsic Seasonality:** The Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE) metric is defined throughout the study utilizing a standard, non-seasonal random walk forecast ($y_{t} = y_{t-1}$) as the naive benchmark denominator.[1] Given that macroeconomic variables such as industrial production and retail sales frequently exhibit powerful residual seasonality despite rigorous statistical agency adjustments [36], utilizing a seasonal random walk ($y_{t} = y_{t-12}$) for the MASE denominator for these specific target variables would provide a far more rigorous and mathematically appropriate scaling factor, aligning the evaluation closer with standard time series econometric practices.
3.  **Covariate Transformation Search Space and Lag Dynamics:** The search space provided to the agent for data transformations explicitly includes log first-differences, percentage changes, and rolling standardizations, but notably appears to lack explicit lag generation (e.g., testing the predictive utility of $t-1$ versus $t-3$ of a specific covariate).[1] In mixed-frequency macro interactions or interactions with delayed pass-through mechanisms (such as the notoriously delayed transmission of monetary policy rates to the unemployment rate), the specific temporal timing of the lag is absolutely critical.[23] The authors should clarify mathematically whether the Chronos-2 group attention mechanism renders explicit lag engineering entirely obsolete by observing the entire context window simultaneously, or if the exclusion of lag parameters was a procedural omission in the design of the agent's search space.
4.  **Chronos-2 Fine-Tuning Loss Function and Data Leakage:** The methodology section states that LoRA fine-tuning is performed only once utilizing the data available at the very first forecast origin, and these static weights are subsequently reused.[1] Later in the analysis, a "periodic retuning" variant is tested annually to rule out weight staleness.[1, 37] The methodology section must rigorously clarify the specific mathematical loss function utilized during this fine-tuning process (e.g., quantile loss versus cross-entropy) and explicitly detail how the target variable boundaries were strictly managed during the expanding window updates to absolutely prevent insidious forward-looking data leakage during the model adaptation phase.
5.  **Ablation Study Granularity by Forecast Horizon:** In Table 6 (Ablation analysis: validation vs. test era), the report demonstrates that the addition of the policy rate covariate increases the validation-to-test performance gap by a massive 14.1%.[1] It would be highly instructive for the reader to decompose this specific algorithmic failure further. Did the inclusion of the policy rate specifically and disproportionately damage the 12-month horizon forecasts during the 2022–2024 rapid tightening cycle, or was the degradation uniform across all forecast horizons ($h \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$)? A horizon-specific, granular breakdown of the ablation table would yield significantly deeper econometric clarity regarding how the models internalize monetary policy shocks.
6.  **Context Window Sensitivity and Deep History:** The LLM agent deliberately selects a 96-month context window, specifically rejecting the "unlimited" context option provided in the search space.[1] The theoretical discussion should critically address whether this arbitrary 8-year truncation acts as an implicit rolling-window adaptation mechanism, allowing the model to intentionally "forget" deep historical macroeconomic regimes (e.g., the structural dynamics of the 2008 global financial crisis) and thereby inadvertently increasing the model's sensitivity to recent local trends, actively contributing to the severe out-of-sample overfitting observed in the post-COVID era.
7.  **Format and Typographical Checks:** Equation (1) contains a minor but confusing notational ambiguity (`month_end(m) + publication_lag lag <= t`). The repetitive inclusion of the term `lag` should be corrected for mathematical precision and clarity.[1] Furthermore, formatting for mathematical variables in-text should consistently utilize standard LaTeX math mode.

### Questions for the authors

To ensure the manuscript achieves the exacting standard of empirical rigor required for publication in the *International Journal of Forecasting*, the authors are requested to address the following specific, highly technical questions in their revision:

1.  How mathematically sensitive is the agent's covariate discovery trajectory to the use of latest-vintage data versus true real-time vintages? If the entire autoresearch loop is executed over true, unrevised real-time data vintages (explicitly accounting for the severe historical preliminary estimation noise inherent in industrial production and retail sales), does the LLM agent still mathematically select the exact same optimal pipeline, or does real-time noise fundamentally alter its optimization trajectory and covariate selection?
2.  Is the empirically observed "overfitting paradox" a universal, architecture-agnostic characteristic of automated macroeconomic pipeline search, or is it merely an algorithmic artifact of the Chronos-2 tokenization and group attention mechanisms? How do alternative, state-of-the-art time series foundation models, specifically those utilizing decoder-only architectures like TimesFM 2.5 or Moirai-2, perform under the identical agentic search protocol and across the same structural breaks?
3.  How does the agent-tuned configuration perform when rigorously evaluated using strict probabilistic scoring rules (e.g., the Continuous Ranked Probability Score or quantile loss functions) rather than solely deterministic point-forecast metrics? Did the LLM-guided optimization successfully improve the calibration and width of the forecast uncertainty bounds during the COVID-19 and post-2022 regime shifts, even as the median point estimates deteriorated?
4.  If the LLM agent is theoretically permitted to conduct its search dynamically (e.g., utilizing rolling or expanding search windows that are algorithmically updated annually throughout the out-of-sample test era), does the agent demonstrate the analytical capacity to autonomously recognize the structural breakdown of the policy rate relationship and adapt the pipeline accordingly by dropping the covariate?
5.  How does the optimization efficiency and the final generalization error of the Claude Sonnet LLM agent compare to a rigorous, mathematically formalized AutoML baseline, such as Bayesian Optimization utilizing Gaussian Processes or Tree-structured Parzen Estimators, operating over the exact same configuration space and constraint set?

### Overall assessment

**Recommendation: Major Revision**

The manuscript addresses a highly relevant, frontier topic positioned squarely at the intersection of automated machine learning (AutoML), artificial intelligence, and applied macroeconomic forecasting.[4, 33] The core empirical finding—that zero-shot time series foundation models exhibit vastly superior robustness to profound structural breaks and regime shifts compared to both classical econometrics and highly optimized, agent-tuned pipelines—is theoretically compelling and practically indispensable for institutional forecasting entities.[3] Furthermore, the adaptation of the "autoresearch" agentic framework to enforce a strict, pseudo-real-time macroeconomic evaluation environment demonstrates a high degree of methodological innovation and ambition.[2, 25] The identification of the "overfitting paradox" within automated search serves as a crucial warning regarding the limitations of LLM-guided optimization in non-stationary domains.

However, the scientific validity, econometric rigor, and broader generalizability of the findings are currently severely constrained by several critical methodological gaps that must be rectified prior to publication. Foremost, the absolute reliance on latest-vintage data rather than true real-time data vintages fundamentally threatens the integrity of the simulated search environment, potentially rewarding the agent for discovering statistical relationships and smoothings that were entirely unobservable to forecasters in real-time.[5, 7] The restriction of the analysis to a single foundation model architecture (Chronos-2) severely limits the broader, generalized applicability of the "overfitting paradox," making it impossible to separate AutoML failure from architectural artifact.[1, 16] Furthermore, the absolute absence of probabilistic evaluation metrics (such as CRPS) overlooks a primary, foundational mandate of modern macroeconomic forecasting, failing to comprehensively assess how the agent impacts uncertainty quantification during crises.[18, 24] Finally, comparing the highly sophisticated LLM agent solely to a trivial random search, rather than established, mathematically rigorous AutoML optimization algorithms, fails to establish the true utility of the language model's semantic reasoning capabilities in this domain.[33]

Addressing these substantive issues will require significant re-computation and structural expansion of the empirical framework. Provided the authors can successfully implement true real-time data robustness checks, incorporate at least one alternative foundation model architectural baseline, rigorously evaluate density forecasts across the structural breaks, and appropriately benchmark the agent against standard mathematical optimization, the manuscript possesses the clear potential to make a significant, robust, and highly cited contribution to the *International Journal of Forecasting*.
