\begin{abstract}
We evaluate whether LLM-guided search can improve pseudo-real-time macroeconomic forecasts from Chronos-2, a time-series foundation model, across three small open economies: Norway, Canada, and Sweden. The search discovers country-specific, economically interpretable covariate pipelines that reduce validation-era MASE by \norValGainPct{} (Norway), \canValGainPct{} (Canada), and \sweValGainPct{} (Sweden). However, out-of-sample generalization is heterogeneous. Norway's gains collapse on the 2016--2025 test period (\norTestGapPct{} degradation), Canada's mostly survive (\canTestGapPct), while Sweden's \emph{improve} further (\sweTestGapPct). The key mechanism is that Norway's search relies on LoRA fine-tuning, which overfits; Sweden's uses covariate selection and context truncation, which generalize. Zero-shot Chronos-2 is the most robust single method across all three countries. Chronos-2's predictive bands under-cover uniformly on these macro series---the nominal $80\%$ band covers only \pooledFTeighty{} of realizations---and fine-tuning does not fix this; a lightweight per-series isotonic recalibration closes most of the gap at the $50\%$ level (\pooledFTfifty{} to \pooledCalfifty{}) and materially narrows it at the $80\%$ level.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} macroeconomic forecasting, foundation models, automated machine learning, time series, small open economies

\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} C53, C55, E37
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ===================================================================

Time-series foundation models---large neural networks pretrained on billions of observations from diverse domains---have rapidly entered the forecaster's toolkit. Models such as Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos,ansari2025chronos2}, TimesFM \citep{das2024decoder}, and MOIRAI \citep{woo2024unified} promise accurate zero-shot forecasts without task-specific training. Whether they can match or exceed classical methods in rigorous macroeconomic applications is an empirical question with no foregone conclusion.

A related question is whether \emph{automated pipeline search} can add value beyond zero-shot inference. Inspired by \citet{karpathy2026autoresearch}, we design an outer loop in which a large language model proposes pipeline configurations---covariates, transformations, context window, and fine-tuning settings---evaluated against a rolling validation metric. The agent may select from 14 macroeconomic covariates and adjust fine-tuning hyperparameters, but cannot modify the model architecture.

We investigate these questions across three small open economies: Norway, Canada, and Sweden. Most economies are small and open, yet the empirical forecasting literature disproportionately evaluates methods on US data \citep{stock2002macroeconomic}. Our three countries share important structural features---independent monetary policy, commodity exposure, high-quality statistical offices---but differ in transmission channels. Norway is an oil-exporting economy; Canada is a large commodity exporter closely tied to the US; Sweden has a diversified manufacturing base oriented toward Europe. Because these economies are unlikely to appear prominently in TSFM pretraining corpora, the zero-shot exercise is a cleaner test of out-of-distribution generalization.

We use a harmonized design across all three countries: four target variables (CPI inflation, industrial production, retail sales, and unemployment) at horizons of 1, 3, 6, and 12 months, evaluated on \nValOrigins{} monthly validation origins (2006--2015) and \nTestOrigins{} test origins (2016--2025). Five search comparators---informed LLM, domain-blind LLM, random, greedy stepwise, and a pre-locked manual economist benchmark---operate over the same action space to isolate the value of LLM reasoning and domain knowledge.

Our main findings are fourfold. First, zero-shot Chronos-2 is competitive with strong classical baselines (ARIMA, BVAR) across all three countries, with test-era MASE of \norZsTest{} (Norway), \canZsTest{} (Canada), and \sweZsTest{} (Sweden). Second, agentic search discovers country-specific, economically interpretable pipelines---monetary policy and exchange rates for Norway, partner-area GDP for Canada, domestic credit and housing for Sweden. Third, the generalization of these gains is \emph{heterogeneous}: Norway's gains collapse on the test period (\norTestGapPct{} degradation), Canada's mostly survive (\canTestGapPct{}), while Sweden's actually \emph{improve} out of sample (\sweTestGapPct{}). Fourth, the mechanism behind this heterogeneity is that Norway's search relies on LoRA fine-tuning, which overfits to validation-era covariate relationships; Sweden's search uses covariate selection and context truncation without fine-tuning, which proves robust to regime change.

This pattern is itself the paper's central contribution. It demonstrates that agentic pipeline search can add value for macro forecasting, but only when the search discovers representation changes (which covariates to include, how much context to use) rather than parameter changes (fine-tuning weights). Fixed-window validation in non-stationary environments risks overfitting, and the risk is concentrated in fine-tuning---not in covariate selection. For practitioners, the implication is clear: prefer covariate and representation search over fine-tuning when regime changes are likely.

\section{Related literature}
\label{sec:literature}
% ===================================================================

This paper connects three literatures: macro forecasting, time-series foundation models, and agentic AutoML.

\subsection{Macroeconomic forecasting}
\label{sec:lit_macro}

Data-rich macro forecasting has progressed from dynamic factor models \citep{stock2002macroeconomic,stock2002forecasting,forni2005generalized} through Bayesian VARs with shrinkage priors \citep{banbura2010large,koop2013large} to machine learning methods that capture non-linearities \citep{coulombe2022machine,babii2024econometrics}. Real-time evaluation must contend with the ragged-edge problem of asynchronous publication \citep{ghysels2004predicting,giannone2008nowcasting}; our pseudo-real-time protocol builds directly on this tradition, using expanding windows and \citet{diebold2002comparing} tests as standard \citep{clark2013advances}.

A consistent finding in the macro forecasting literature is that simple models are hard to beat, especially during periods of structural instability. Forecasting small open economies poses particular challenges: oil shocks, exchange rate volatility, and trade-channel disruptions can alter covariate relationships rapidly \citep{bjornland2019dutch,aastveit2023inflation}. Our cross-country design tests whether automated covariate discovery can adapt to these country-specific transmission channels.

\subsection{Foundation models for time series}
\label{sec:lit_tsfm}

Time-series foundation models (TSFMs) are pretrained on massive, heterogeneous corpora to enable zero-shot inference on unseen series. Chronos \citep{ansari2024chronos} treats forecasting as language modeling via value quantization; its successor Chronos-2 \citep{ansari2025chronos2} adds group attention that alternates between within-series and cross-series patterns, enabling in-context learning from covariates without architectural changes. Alternative TSFMs include TimesFM \citep{das2024decoder,das2025incontext}, Lag-Llama \citep{rasul2023lagllama}, and MOIRAI \citep{woo2024unified,liu2025moirai2}; concerns about pretraining data contamination are documented by \citet{aksu2024benchmarking}.

The tension between zero-shot inference and task-specific fine-tuning is central to applied TSFM work \citep{rasul2023lagllama}. Our research interrogates this tradeoff by decomposing forecast gains into the foundation model itself, economy-specific adaptation, and the additional value of agent-guided search---across three countries that each present different adaptation challenges.

Separate from point accuracy, the calibration of predictive bands emitted by TSFMs has received much less systematic attention than in classical and ML forecasting, where conformal prediction \citep{romano2019conformalized,angelopoulos2023conformal} and isotonic recalibration \citep{kuleshov2018accurate} are standard post-hoc remedies. To our knowledge, no prior work has documented TSFM band calibration on small-open-economy macroeconomic series, nor evaluated per-series conformal-style recalibration in that setting; Section~\ref{sec:calibration} takes a first step.

\subsection{Automated machine learning and feature engineering}
\label{sec:lit_automl}

Traditional AutoML frameworks \citep{feurer2015automl,erickson2020autogluon,shchur2023autogluon} employ Bayesian optimization and meta-learning but operate within rigidly defined search spaces and cannot incorporate domain knowledge. A newer paradigm uses LLMs as meta-optimizers that reason about results and autonomously modify experimental configurations \citep{lu2024aiscientist,yamada2025ai,cheng2026agentic,dong2025dataforge}.

Our approach builds on Karpathy's ``autoresearch'' framework \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch}, which uses a ratchet loop with fixed data preparation, a mutable training script, and a natural-language specification of the objective. This has been adopted across disciplines \citep{oconnell2026signal,weidener2026rethinking,liu2026autoresearchclaw} but not yet in macroeconomic forecasting. We adapt it by constraining the search to data representations, covariate selection, and fine-tuning---never model architecture---with pseudo-real-time evaluation governed by rolling forecast-origin validation loss.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ===================================================================

\subsection{Foundation model: Chronos-2}
\label{sec:chronos}

We use Chronos-2 \citep{ansari2025chronos2} as the foundation model backbone, accessed via the AutoGluon TimeSeries interface \citep{shchur2023autogluon}. The model has 120 million parameters and supports a maximum context length of 8,192 time steps. Its group attention mechanism alternates between time attention (within a single series) and group attention (across series in a batch), enabling in-context learning from covariates without requiring explicit covariate-specific input channels.

Chronos-2 natively integrates both past covariates and known future covariates during inference. In our implementation, all macroeconomic covariates are provided as \emph{past covariates only}: the model receives historical values up to the forecast origin but no future values. This is critical for pseudo-real-time validity.

\paragraph{Fine-tuning.} Chronos-2 supports parameter-efficient adaptation via Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), targeting the self-attention Q, K, V, O projections and the output patch embedding with rank $r = 8$ and $\alpha = 16$. Fine-tuning is performed once on the data available at the first forecast origin, and the adapted model is reused for all subsequent origins. This fit-once architecture means the fine-tuned model may overfit to early-period patterns---a limitation we return to in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Agentic search procedure}
\label{sec:search}

The search is orchestrated by an outer loop that uses a large language model (Claude Sonnet, via the Anthropic API) to propose pipeline configurations. The loop follows a ratchet design inspired by \citet{karpathy2026autoresearch}:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{LLM-guided pipeline search}
\label{alg:search}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Establish baseline: evaluate zero-shot Chronos-2 on all \nValOrigins{} validation origins
\For{$i = 1, 2, \ldots, N$}
    \State LLM reads: task description, search space, current best config, iteration history
    \State LLM proposes a JSON configuration (covariates, transforms, context length, fine-tuning)
    \State \textbf{Quick evaluation:} run on 20 subsampled origins
    \If{quick score $<$ best quick score so far}
        \State \textbf{Full evaluation:} run on all \nValOrigins{} origins
        \If{full score $<$ current best}
            \State \textbf{Accept:} update best configuration and score
        \Else
            \State \textbf{Reject}
        \EndIf
    \Else
        \State \textbf{Reject}
    \EndIf
    \State Log iteration (config, scores, status) to experiment log
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The two-phase evaluation balances iteration speed with evaluation fidelity. The primary metric is the mean absolute scaled error (MASE), averaged across all target variables and all four forecast horizons.

\paragraph{Prompt design.} The search is run independently for each country. In the \emph{informed} variant, the LLM receives a country-specific prompt containing domain knowledge (e.g., ``Norway is an oil-exporting economy; Brent crude and exchange rates are highly relevant''). In the \emph{domain-blind} variant, the prompt is stripped of all country-specific hints---variable names are generic. This allows us to isolate the value of domain knowledge in the search.

\paragraph{Search comparators.} We compare five search strategies operating over the same action space: (i) informed LLM search, (ii) domain-blind LLM search, (iii) random search (uniform sampling), (iv) greedy stepwise search (deterministic, non-LLM), and (v) a pre-locked manual economist benchmark (covariates chosen by the authors before seeing results).

\section{Data and evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:data}
% ===================================================================

\subsection{Three-country macroeconomic panels}
\label{sec:panel}

We construct harmonized monthly panels for Norway, Canada, and Sweden, each following a common template of four target variables and 14 candidate covariates. The four targets are CPI inflation (12-month rate of change), industrial production (seasonally adjusted), retail sales (volume index, seasonally adjusted), and the unemployment rate (LFS, seasonally adjusted).\footnote{Sweden's retail sales series from Statistics Sweden begins only in 2023 and is excluded from the analysis. Sweden results are computed over three targets (CPI, industrial production, unemployment). See the online appendix for details.}

The 14 covariates span domestic indicators (house prices, credit, exports, imports, the policy rate, bilateral exchange rates against EUR and USD) and global variables (Brent crude, a broad equity index, the federal funds rate, US CPI, VIX, global economic policy uncertainty, and a partner-area activity variable). The partner-area variable is euro-area GDP for Norway and Sweden, and US GDP for Canada, reflecting different trade orientations.

Data sources are country-specific: Statistics Norway (SSB) and Norges Bank for Norway; Statistics Canada and the Bank of Canada for Canada; Statistics Sweden (SCB) and the Riksbank for Sweden. Global variables are sourced from FRED for all three countries. The full variable catalog, including series identifiers and publication lags, is provided in the project repository.

\subsection{Pseudo-real-time discipline}
\label{sec:realtime}

At each forecast origin $t$, we restrict the information set to data that would have been publicly available on date $t$. For a monthly observation indexed at month-end $m$, the observation is available when
\begin{equation}
    \text{month\_end}(m) + \text{publication\_lag} \leq t.
\end{equation}
Publication lags are configured per country and per variable based on official release schedules. Lags range from 0 days (policy rates, known in real time) to 90 days (euro-area GDP). Daily series are aggregated to monthly averages; quarterly series are forward-filled. We use latest-vintage data throughout but restrict availability by publication lag. This is pseudo-real-time, not true real-time with data vintages.

\subsection{Evaluation design}
\label{sec:evaluation}

We use a rolling expanding-window design. For each forecast origin $t$, the model receives all data available up to $t$ and produces point forecasts at horizons $h \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$ months ahead for each target variable.

\paragraph{Eras.} The validation era spans January 2006 to December 2015 (\nValOrigins{} monthly origins), used for search and model selection. The test era spans January 2016 to March 2025 (\nTestOrigins{} origins), evaluated with the pipeline frozen from the search phase. The evaluation window is common across all three countries. We further decompose the test era into three subperiods: pre-COVID (2016--2019), COVID (2020--2021), and post-COVID/inflation (2022--2025).

\paragraph{Metrics.} The mean absolute scaled error (MASE) is defined as $\text{MASE}_h = \text{MAE}_h / \text{MAE}_h^{\text{RW}}$, where $\text{MAE}_h^{\text{RW}}$ is the MAE of the $h$-step-ahead random walk forecast. The random walk scores exactly 1.000 by construction. MASE is the primary metric because it is scale-independent across targets with different units.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{sec:baselines}

We evaluate a comprehensive set of baselines:

\paragraph{Univariate baselines.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Random walk:} $\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = y_t$.
    \item \textbf{Seasonal naive:} $\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = y_{t+h-12}$.
    \item \textbf{AR($p$):} Direct autoregressive regression, lag order selected by BIC over $p \in \{1, \ldots, 12\}$.
    \item \textbf{ARIMA:} Order selected by AIC over $p \in \{0,1,2,3\}$, $d \in \{0,1\}$, $q \in \{0,1,2\}$.
    \item \textbf{ETS:} Exponential smoothing with automatic model selection by AIC.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Multivariate baselines.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,resume]
    \item \textbf{VAR:} Vector autoregression on the target plus the top covariates from the respective country's search. Lag order selected by BIC.
    \item \textbf{Factor model:} Principal components from all 14 covariates used as regressors in direct $h$-step forecasting equations.
    \item \textbf{BVAR:} Minnesota-shrinkage Bayesian VAR, tuned on validation data.
    \item \textbf{Elastic net:} Direct forecasting with lagged target and lagged covariates, penalty tuned by cross-validation.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Foundation model methods.}
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,resume]
    \item \textbf{Chronos-2 (zero-shot):} No covariates, no fine-tuning.
    \item \textbf{Chronos-2 (agent-tuned):} Best configuration from the informed LLM search.
    \item \textbf{Chronos-2 (manual):} Pre-locked economist benchmark---covariates chosen by the authors before seeing results, not revised after.
\end{enumerate}

All baselines are re-estimated at each forecast origin using only data available at that origin, ensuring full pseudo-real-time compliance.

\paragraph{Statistical inference.} We test for equal predictive ability using the \citet{diebold2002comparing} test with Newey-West HAC standard errors (bandwidth $h - 1$).

\subsection{Predictive band calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

The results so far evaluate point forecasts under MASE. Practitioners using macroeconomic forecasts, however, care about predictive bands as much as point estimates: a central-bank projection without a fan chart is of limited operational use. Chronos-2 returns a discrete predictive distribution at each origin as a set of five quantiles $\tau \in \{0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9\}$, which map directly to nominal $80\%$ (q10--q90) and $50\%$ (q25--q75) prediction intervals. Whether these bands are empirically calibrated---that is, whether $80\%$ of realized outcomes actually fall inside the nominal $80\%$ band---is a separate question from whether the median is accurate. We devote this subsection to that question, because an otherwise accurate point forecast with miscalibrated bands can still mislead downstream risk assessments.

\paragraph{Methodology.}
We measure empirical coverage for each nominal level $\alpha \in \{0.5, 0.8\}$, pooled across all \nCountries{} countries, target variables, forecast horizons $h \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$, and \nTestOrigins{} test-era origins. For the recalibration exercise, we follow a quantile-based variant of the isotonic recalibration scheme of \citet{kuleshov2018accurate} that is equivalent in spirit to split-conformal quantile regression \citep{romano2019conformalized} applied per series. Concretely, for each (country, target, horizon) triple we compute the probability-integral-transform (PIT) values on the \emph{validation} era and take the empirical CDF $\hat{F}$ of those PIT values. At test time, the calibrated quantile forecast at level $\tau$ is $Q_{\mathrm{cal}}(\tau) = Q_{\mathrm{base}}(\hat{F}^{-1}(\tau))$, where $Q_{\mathrm{base}}$ is the linearly interpolated base quantile function produced by Chronos-2 from its five emitted quantiles. This procedure is local to each series, requires no retraining of the foundation model, and uses validation-era residuals only---fully respecting the pseudo-real-time discipline. It targets marginal, not conditional, calibration.

\paragraph{Universal under-coverage.}
Table~\ref{tab:calibration} reports per-country, per-target coverage for the $80\%$ and $50\%$ bands, for the fine-tuned agent-selected configuration (``FT''), the zero-shot baseline (``ZS''), and the recalibrated fine-tuned output (``Cal''). The under-coverage is uniform: pooled across the full test set, the fine-tuned $80\%$ band covers only \pooledFTeighty{} of realizations against a nominal $0.80$ (a gap of \pooledFTeightyGap{}~pp), and the $50\%$ band covers \pooledFTfifty{} against nominal $0.50$ (a gap of \pooledFTfiftyGap{}~pp). The zero-shot bands are marginally tighter still, at \pooledZSeighty{} and \pooledZSfifty{} respectively (gaps \pooledZSeightyGap{} and \pooledZSfiftyGap{}~pp). Under-coverage is therefore not an artifact of LoRA adaptation---the raw pretrained Chronos-2 predictive distribution is itself variance-compressed on these macro series, and fine-tuning neither fixes nor meaningfully worsens the problem (less than one percentage point of difference in either band).

\paragraph{Directional biases are separable from compression.}
Pooled coverage gaps conceal a richer story that emerges only when the PIT distribution is examined per series. Table~\ref{tab:calibration_bias} reports the fraction of realizations above the q90 quantile and below the q10 quantile for each (country, target) pair under zero-shot Chronos-2; under perfect calibration each fraction should equal $0.10$. Figure~\ref{fig:pit_histograms} plots the full PIT histograms, with the two outer tail bins highlighted. Three qualitatively distinct patterns appear. First, some series are \emph{right-tail heavy}: \worstRightCountry{} \worstRightTarget{} places \worstRightFrac{} of actuals above q90 against a nominal $0.10$, and Sweden CPI and industrial production exhibit similar (though milder) right-tail excess, consistent with Chronos-2 underestimating the upside of the 2021--2024 inflation and output-rebound episodes. Second, some series are \emph{left-tail heavy}: \worstLeftCountry{} \worstLeftTarget{} has \worstLeftFrac{} of realizations below q10, reflecting a steep 2020--2021 decline in the unemployment rate that the model did not anticipate. Third, some series show essentially symmetric compression: Norway CPI has close to equal mass in both tails, indicating that the base model is simply too confident rather than systematically biased. This decomposition---compression versus directional bias---matters because the two pathologies call for different remedies. Isotonic recalibration, which is monotone in $\tau$, can correct both compression and bias that is well-approximated by a constant quantile-level shift in the validation era, but cannot correct conditional biases that depend on covariates or regime.

\paragraph{Recalibration results.}
The ``Cal'' column of Table~\ref{tab:calibration} shows the effect of per-series isotonic recalibration. Pooled empirical coverage of the $80\%$ band moves from \pooledFTeighty{} to \pooledCaleighty{}, narrowing the nominal--empirical gap from \pooledFTeightyGap{} to \pooledCaleightyGap{}~pp (about $28\%$ of the gap closed). The $50\%$ band improves more sharply: from \pooledFTfifty{} to \pooledCalfifty{}, narrowing the gap from \pooledFTfiftyGap{} to \pooledCalfiftyGap{}~pp (about $68\%$ closed). The asymmetry between the two bands is structural. Because the base forecast emits only five quantiles, $\hat{F}^{-1}(\tau)$ saturates at $\tau=0.1$ and $\tau=0.9$---there is no base-model support beyond those endpoints for the calibrator to stretch into. Recalibration therefore cannot push an $80\%$ band's coverage all the way to $0.80$ when the base distribution already exhausts its tail reach; it can, however, push the $50\%$ band close to nominal because both $0.25$ and $0.75$ remain interior to the base-quantile grid. A denser base quantile grid would lift this ceiling.

Per-series, recalibration is strongest where the validation-era PIT distribution resembles the test-era PIT distribution, and weakest under regime change. Sweden CPI and Norway retail sales each gain roughly $8$--$10$~pp of $80\%$-band coverage; these series show similar tail behaviour in 2006--2015 and 2016--2025. Canada unemployment, in contrast, \emph{loses} approximately $8$~pp of coverage after recalibration, because the validation-era residuals reflect a labour market that tightened smoothly over 2006--2015 while the test era includes the sharp 2020 spike and 2021 recovery. The heterogeneity parallels the point-forecast generalization finding of Section~\ref{sec:results_gap}: any recalibrator fit to one regime carries a regime-shift risk that no method blind to structural breaks can resolve.

Three findings from this exercise recur in the discussion that follows. First, Chronos-2's predictive bands are uniformly too narrow on these macro series, by roughly ten percentage points at the $80\%$ level. Second, because the zero-shot and fine-tuned variants under-cover to within one percentage point of one another, variance compression is a property of the pretrained foundation model, not of our LoRA adaptation---and, symmetrically, fine-tuning cannot be relied upon to fix band calibration. Third, a lightweight per-series isotonic post-hoc recalibrator closes most of the $50\%$-band gap and a non-trivial fraction of the $80\%$-band gap at essentially no computational cost, provided the validation and deployment regimes are reasonably aligned. Calibrated bands should therefore accompany any point forecast from a TSFM of this class.


% ===================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ===================================================================

\paragraph{Zero-shot robustness.}
Zero-shot Chronos-2 is the most robust single method across all three countries. Its test-era MASE is below or near 1.0 everywhere, meaning it matches or beats the random walk---a benchmark that is itself hard to outperform during regime changes. Pretraining on diverse time series provides implicit regularization against overfitting to specific covariate relationships. For practitioners, zero-shot foundation models should be a standard baseline in macroeconomic forecasting.

\paragraph{When does agentic search help?}
The answer is conditional on mechanism. Covariate selection and context truncation (as in Sweden) generalize because they change the model's \emph{input representation} without altering its parameters. LoRA fine-tuning (as in Norway) adapts model weights to validation-era patterns that may not persist. This is consistent with the machine learning principle that representation learning is more robust than parameter tuning on small datasets---and 120 monthly origins is a small dataset by any standard.

\paragraph{Domain knowledge.}
Informed LLM search finds more interpretable pipelines than domain-blind search in all countries. But interpretability does not guarantee robustness: Norway's economically interpretable covariates (oil, policy rate, exchange rate) still overfit. The blind agent discovers risk and equity proxies (S\&P 500, VIX) that the informed agent misses. For the paper's central question, domain knowledge narrows the search toward macro-relevant covariates but does not protect against validation-era overfitting.

\paragraph{Country heterogeneity.}
The three countries present different generalization patterns that would be invisible in a single-country study. Had we evaluated only Norway, the conclusion would be that agentic search is counterproductive. Had we evaluated only Sweden, the conclusion would be that agentic search delivers large, robust gains. The cross-country evidence reveals the conditional nature of the result: \emph{what} the search discovers matters more than whether it discovers something.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We use a single TSFM (Chronos-2); results may differ for TimesFM or MOIRAI. Our pseudo-real-time protocol uses latest-vintage data, not true real-time with data revisions. Our density evaluation in Section~\ref{sec:calibration} uses Chronos-2's five emitted quantiles; a richer quantile grid or a continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) could reveal finer patterns, particularly in the tails where our isotonic recalibrator saturates. Sweden evaluates three targets (retail sales excluded due to a data gap); cross-country MASE averages are not perfectly comparable. The search budget of 50 iterations may not exhaust the search space, though diminishing returns are observed in all countries.


% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ===================================================================

We evaluate agentic search over covariates, context window, and fine-tuning settings for Chronos-2 across three small open economies. The search discovers country-specific, economically interpretable pipelines in all three countries. However, out-of-sample generalization is heterogeneous: fine-tuning-based gains collapse under regime change, while representation-based gains survive. Zero-shot Chronos-2 is the most robust single method.

Two practical implications follow. First, zero-shot foundation models should be standard baselines in macro forecasting---their robustness to structural breaks is a concrete advantage. Second, when using automated search, prefer covariate selection and context window adjustments over fine-tuning in non-stationary environments. The cross-country evidence shows that \emph{what} the search discovers is more important than \emph{whether} it discovers something. Future work should explore rolling validation windows, multiple TSFMs, and extension to additional economies.

Beyond point accuracy, we document that Chronos-2's predictive bands are systematically too narrow on these three economies---the pooled $80\%$ band covers only \pooledFTeighty{} of realizations, and the $50\%$ band only \pooledFTfifty{}---and that fine-tuning neither causes nor corrects this compression. A per-series isotonic recalibrator fit to validation-era PIT residuals closes roughly $28\%$ of the $80\%$-band gap and roughly $68\%$ of the $50\%$-band gap, with the remaining $80\%$-band shortfall attributable to the base model's finite quantile support. The practical takeaway mirrors the point-forecast message: zero-shot foundation models are robust but under-confident, and the right response is to accompany point forecasts with a lightweight, locally-fit calibration layer rather than to trust raw TSFM quantile output.
