# Referee Report: "Can Agentic Search Improve Foundation Model Forecasts for Small Open Economies?"

### Summary

The manuscript titled "Can Agentic Search Improve Foundation Model Forecasts for Small Open Economies?" presents a highly ambitious, timely, and computationally sophisticated investigation into the intersection of large language model (LLM) agentic workflows, time series foundation models (TSFMs), and macroeconomic forecasting. The authors evaluate whether an autonomous, LLM-guided search procedure can iteratively optimize a forecasting pipeline for a small open economy (Norway) by autonomously selecting covariates, applying data transformations, modifying context windows, and tuning hyperparameters. Utilizing Chronos-2 (120M parameters) as the foundational backbone and an evaluation framework built around a pseudo-real-time expanding window protocol spanning from 2006 to 2025, the study documents several compelling findings.[1, 2, 3] Foremost among these is the discovery that an LLM agent successfully identifies an economically intuitive configuration—specifically leveraging Brent crude oil prices, the Norges Bank policy rate, United States inflation, and the NOK/EUR exchange rate—that reduces validation-era Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE) by 6.8% relative to the baseline.[1] 

However, the manuscript's most profound contribution lies in its documentation of what can be termed an "overfitting paradox" within automated pipeline optimization. The agent-tuned configuration, despite its theoretical coherence and superior in-sample performance during the stable 2006–2015 macroeconomic regime, suffers from severe out-of-sample overfitting during subsequent structural breaks.[1] When confronted with the unprecedented macroeconomic volatility of the COVID-19 pandemic and the aggressive global monetary tightening cycle of 2022–2024, the tuned model's fixed covariate relationships actively mislead the forecasting outputs. Conversely, the zero-shot, un-tuned Chronos-2 model demonstrates remarkable robustness to these structural breaks, significantly outperforming both the agent-tuned pipeline and classical univariate baselines at longer forecast horizons.[1, 4] This observation—that the broad, domain-agnostic pre-training of zero-shot TSFMs acts as a form of implicit regularization against regime-specific overfitting—is a highly valuable insight with immediate practical implications for central banks, institutional forecasters, and the broader econometric community.[5, 6]

The manuscript addresses a highly relevant question perfectly aligned with the aims and scope of the *International Journal of Forecasting* (IJF). The application of Karpathy's "autoresearch" ratchet-loop paradigm [7, 8] to macroeconomic pipeline optimization represents a novel methodological bridge between the rapidly advancing field of automated machine learning (AutoML) and traditional macroeconomic forecasting. Furthermore, the rigorous attempt to subject these novel foundation models to the discipline of pseudo-out-of-sample evaluation demonstrates a commendable commitment to empirical rigor.[1] Nevertheless, despite the novelty of the premise and the critical importance of the findings, the manuscript in its current form suffers from several substantial methodological and econometric deficiencies. Chief among these are the omission of appropriate multivariate macroeconomic baselines, an absence of formal statistical testing for predictive accuracy, an oversimplified approach to the fine-tuning architecture that artificially handicaps the agent-tuned model, and a highly problematic reliance on "latest-vintage" data rather than true real-time data vintages. The empirical setup requires significant reinforcement to meet the rigorous evaluation standards expected by the IJF. The following sections detail the major substantive issues that must be addressed prior to publication consideration, followed by minor technical corrections and specific questions for the authors.

### Major comments

**1. The Asymmetry of the Baseline Suite and the Omission of Multivariate Benchmarks**
The core empirical claim of the manuscript rests on evaluating the performance of the agent-tuned Chronos-2 model against the zero-shot Chronos-2 model and a suite of classical baselines (Random Walk, Seasonal Naive, AR(p), ARIMA, and ETS).[1] This evaluation design introduces a fundamental structural asymmetry that critically undermines the validity of the comparative performance claims. The agent-tuned model is explicitly permitted to ingest a high-dimensional, multivariate information set, eventually selecting up to four exogenous macroeconomic covariates (Brent crude, the policy rate, US CPI, and the NOK/EUR exchange rate) to inform its predictions.[1] In stark contrast, every classical baseline selected by the authors is strictly univariate, constrained to forecasting the target variable using only its own historical lags. 

In the macroeconomic forecasting literature, it has been unequivocally established for over two decades that incorporating large, diverse datasets improves forecast accuracy, provided that the curse of dimensionality is appropriately managed through regularization, shrinkage, or dimensionality reduction techniques.[9, 10] By comparing a multivariate foundation model exclusively against univariate autoregressive models, the manuscript fails to isolate the value-added of the TSFM architecture or the agentic search procedure. It is impossible to determine whether the foundation model is outperforming the classical models because of its advanced neural architecture (e.g., the group attention mechanism) [3] or simply because it has access to vastly more information. For a manuscript submitted to the IJF, the inclusion of state-of-the-art multivariate econometric benchmarks that ingest the exact same 14-variable covariate pool is absolutely mandatory. 

To ensure a scientifically rigorous and balanced evaluation, the authors must implement the following multivariate baselines:
*   **Large Bayesian Vector Autoregressions (BVAR):** The BVAR framework, particularly utilizing Minnesota-type priors, represents the contemporary gold standard for handling medium-to-large macroeconomic datasets without suffering from overfitting.[1, 11] The performance of the agent-tuned Chronos-2 model must be directly benchmarked against a BVAR that is permitted to utilize the identical set of 14 candidate covariates. Furthermore, given the explicit focus on structural breaks (the COVID-19 pandemic and the 2022 inflation surge), estimating a BVAR with stochastic volatility (BVAR-SV) would provide a highly robust, heteroskedasticity-aware benchmark against which to judge the foundation model's performance.[11]
*   **Dynamic Factor Models (DFM):** The manuscript correctly cites the seminal work of Stock and Watson (2002) and Forni et al. (2005) [1, 9], yet conspicuously fails to implement a factor model baseline. Extracting the first few principal components from the 14-variable covariate pool and utilizing them in a Factor-Augmented Vector Autoregression (FAVAR) or a standard DFM is essential. This will demonstrate whether the LLM agent's discrete covariate selection mechanism is superior to continuous, variance-maximizing dimensionality reduction.
*   **Institutional Forecast Combination Benchmarks:** Given that the empirical laboratory is the Norwegian macroeconomy, the manuscript would benefit immensely from benchmarking against established institutional methodologies. Norges Bank has historically utilized sophisticated forecast combination systems, such as the System for Averaging Models (SAM) and its successor SMART, which aggregate predictions from hundreds of distinct statistical models.[12, 13, 14, 15] While replicating the entire SAM infrastructure is unnecessary, introducing a simple equal-weighted or inverse-MSE-weighted forecast combination of various VAR and factor models would provide a highly realistic benchmark that mimics central bank best practices.[16]

**2. The Absence of Formal Statistical Tests for Predictive Accuracy**
A profound methodological gap in the manuscript is the complete absence of formal statistical testing to evaluate the significance of the observed forecast accuracy differentials. Throughout Section 5, the authors report raw differences in Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE).[1] For instance, the authors highlight a 6.8% reduction in MASE during the validation era and note the deterioration of the agent-tuned configuration in the post-COVID period. However, in contemporary macroeconomic forecasting literature, comparing point estimates of loss functions without accompanying tests of statistical significance is considered insufficient.[17, 18] The fact that $\text{RMSE}_{\text{Model A}} < \text{RMSE}_{\text{Model B}}$ in a specific finite sample realization does not inherently prove that Model A is superior in population; the result may simply be an artifact of sample variance.[18]

To align with the rigorous standards of the IJF, the manuscript must be substantially revised to incorporate formal tests of equal predictive accuracy. The implementation of the Diebold-Mariano (DM) test (Diebold and Mariano, 1995) or the Giacomini-White (2006) test of conditional predictive ability is an absolute requirement.[19, 20] 

The implementation of these tests must adhere to several critical econometric nuances:
*   **Autocorrelation in Multi-step Forecasts:** The manuscript evaluates forecasts at horizons $h \in \{1, 3, 6, 12\}$.[1] For any forecast horizon $h > 1$, optimal forecast errors will mechanically exhibit serial correlation following an MA(h-1) process. Consequently, the standard error of the loss differential in the DM test must be estimated using a heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation consistent (HAC) variance estimator, such as the Newey-West or Bartlett kernel.[21] The current draft is entirely silent on the statistical significance of any reported gains, masking whether the foundation model's outperformance at $h=12$ is statistically robust or merely noise.
*   **The Problem of Nested Models:** A unique econometric challenge arises when comparing the zero-shot Chronos-2 model against the agent-tuned Chronos-2 model. Because the agent-tuned version is essentially the zero-shot architecture augmented with specifically selected covariates and fine-tuned LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) weights, the two models are fundamentally nested. As documented in the econometrics literature, the standard DM test statistic limits to zero under the null hypothesis of equal predictive accuracy for nested models when evaluated over an expanding estimation window.[19] Because the authors employ a rolling/expanding hybrid design, they must explicitly articulate their assumptions regarding the asymptotic distribution of their chosen test statistic. Utilizing the Giacomini-White (2006) test, which remains asymptotically valid for nested models under rolling window estimation, is strongly recommended.[19]
*   **Testing with the MASE Statistic:** The authors state that MASE is the primary metric optimized by the LLM agent during the search phase, yet the out-of-sample tables primarily report RMSE.[1] This disconnect is problematic. Furthermore, if the authors wish to test for significant differences in MASE, they must recognize that while the DM statistic for squared errors is standard, the DM statistic calculated over scaled absolute errors (MASE) has been empirically shown to approximate a normal distribution, making it highly suitable for robust hypothesis testing without the distortionary effects associated with metrics like MAPE.[20, 22, 23] The authors must report DM p-values for the MASE differentials between the competing models across all subperiods and variables.

Without the inclusion of robust statistical testing, the manuscript's narrative regarding the superiority or failure of specific models during structural breaks remains empirically unsupported.

**3. The Critical Distinction Between "Pseudo-Real-Time" and True Real-Time Data**
In Section 4.2 ("Pseudo-real-time discipline"), the manuscript articulates its data handling protocol: "We use latest-vintage data throughout but restrict availability by publication lag. This is pseudo-real-time, not true real-time with data vintages. Investigating the additional effect of data revisions is left for future work.".[1] For an empirical forecasting paper submitted to a premier journal, this methodological compromise represents a critical vulnerability. 

The extensive literature on real-time macroeconomic forecasting, pioneered by Croushore and Stark (2001) and heavily expanded over the last two decades, unequivocally demonstrates that evaluating forecasting models using latest-vintage data fundamentally distorts performance metrics.[24, 25, 26, 27] Macroeconomic variables, particularly National Accounts data such as Industrial Production and Retail Sales, undergo substantial and repeated revisions over time.[28] These revisions are not merely noise; they often reflect structural re-benchmarking, the incorporation of delayed census data, and changes in statistical agency methodologies.[26]

By utilizing a 2026 data vintage to evaluate a forecast generated at an origin in 2010, the authors are inadvertently providing the forecasting model with "smoothed" or structurally revised data that a practitioner standing in 2010 could never have observed. Simulating publication lags (e.g., shifting an observation forward by 30 days) addresses the "ragged edge" problem of asynchronous releases, but it completely ignores the measurement error inherent in initial data releases.[9, 29] A model trained and evaluated on latest-vintage data often learns to predict the statistical agency's eventual revisions rather than the actual state of the economy at the time the forecast was made.[26]

This methodological choice is particularly perplexing given that the authors themselves have previously published highly regarded empirical work on Norwegian macroeconomic forecasting explicitly utilizing true real-time data vintages to measure the predictive power of unstructured news media (e.g., Larsen and Thorsrud, 2020; Ellingsen, Larsen, and Thorsrud, 2022).[30, 31, 32] The regression to latest-vintage data in this manuscript is a significant step backward.

The authors must pursue one of the following two corrective paths prior to publication:
*   **Path A (Strongly Recommended):** Re-execute the entire evaluation protocol utilizing a true real-time database with actual historical vintages. Real-time data vintages for the Norwegian macroeconomy are readily accessible via Norges Bank and Statistics Norway. Evaluating the agentic search procedure on true first-release data will exponentially increase the paper's contribution to the applied forecasting literature.
*   **Path B (Acceptable Alternative):** Provide a rigorous, quantitative empirical justification demonstrating why data revisions for these specific 18 variables (Table 6) are negligible enough to safely ignore. This would require a dedicated subsection analyzing the variance, mean revision size, and correlation between first-release and final-release data for the specific target variables. If the revisions are proven to be statistically indistinguishable from white noise, the latest-vintage assumption may be conditionally accepted, though Path A remains vastly superior.

**4. Flaws in the Fine-Tuning Protocol and the Resulting "Overfitting Paradox"**
The manuscript's most prominent narrative hook is the documentation of the "overfitting paradox"—the finding that the agent-tuned pipeline works exceptionally well in-sample (2006–2015) but fails catastrophically out-of-sample (2016–2025) compared to the zero-shot Chronos-2 model.[1] The temporal evolution of forecast error (RMSE) for both the Zero-Shot and Agent-Tuned Chronos-2 models across the test era reveals a critical divergence. While the Agent-Tuned model performs adequately pre-2020, its fixed covariate relationships actively degrade performance during subsequent structural breaks, specifically the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020 and the post-2022 inflation surge. During the post-COVID (2022+) subperiod, the zero-shot model maintains an RMSE of 1.734 at $h=12$, whereas the agent-tuned model's error balloons to an astonishing 3.011.[1] 

While the authors attribute this failure to the breakdown of macroeconomic relationships during regime shifts, a closer inspection of the methodology suggests that this result may be largely an artifact of an artificially handicapped fine-tuning protocol. In Section 3.1, the authors state: "In our setup, fine-tuning is performed once on the data available at the first forecast origin, and the adapted model is reused for all subsequent origins. This fit-once architecture means the fine-tuned model may overfit to early-period patterns.".[1]

This "fit-once" architectural decision is indefensible in the context of rigorous pseudo-real-time macroeconomic forecasting. No practitioner would estimate econometric parameters, or fine-tune the LoRA weights of a large neural network [33], in December 2015 and hold those exact weights frozen for 10 years through a global pandemic, a supply-chain crisis, and a massive energy shock.[34] Classical econometric models, such as the AR(p) and ARIMA baselines utilized in this study, are inherently re-estimated at every single rolling origin.[1, 26] By forcing the agent-tuned Chronos-2 model to hold its parameter weights archaic and frozen for 111 out-of-sample forecast origins, the authors virtually guarantee its obsolescence and subsequent failure.

To make a scientifically valid claim regarding the tension between "task-specific adaptation versus zero-shot robustness," the fine-tuning protocol must be dynamic. The authors must re-run the LoRA fine-tuning step periodically throughout the test era (e.g., recursively re-tuning the network weights annually, or at every 12th forecast origin, utilizing the newly available expanding window of data). Only if a recursively updated, continually learning agent-tuned model *still* underperforms the zero-shot foundation model during the 2022+ regime can the authors definitively claim that zero-shot robustness universally dominates domain-specific adaptation in the presence of structural breaks. As it stands, the agent-tuned model fails primarily because its internal parameters are a decade out of date, not necessarily because the covariates themselves (e.g., oil prices, exchange rates) are inherently misleading.

**5. The Agentic Search Objective Function and Metric Stability**
The optimization ratchet-loop orchestrated by the Claude Sonnet LLM, as described in Algorithm 1, relies on the Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE), averaged comprehensively across all four target variables and all four forecast horizons, as the singular, monolithic reward signal for the search agent.[1, 8] While the MASE is widely acknowledged as an excellent scale-independent metric for comparing forecast accuracy across disparate time series [22, 35, 36], condensing the entire complex performance profile of a multifaceted economy into a single scalar value masks underlying model dynamics and likely induces suboptimal pipeline configurations.

*   **Variable-Specific Divergence:** In the discussion section (Section 6), the authors briefly concede an alarming detail: "the agent-tuned configuration helps unemployment but hurts retail sales".[1] This highlights a fatal flaw in the global averaging objective function. If the LLM agent is optimizing a global average loss, it may be actively destroying forecasting performance in highly volatile series (such as Retail Sales or Industrial Production) in order to achieve marginal, incremental gains in highly persistent, low-variance series (such as the Unemployment rate). The agentic search procedure should not force a single, universal pipeline configuration onto the entire economy. The authors must decompose the search and allow the LLM agent to build four separate, distinct pipeline configurations—one for each macroeconomic target variable. A pipeline configuration optimized for forecasting inflation (e.g., heavily weighting US CPI and global oil prices) is fundamentally incompatible with the optimal configuration for forecasting domestic retail sales.
*   **The Omission of Probabilistic Evaluation:** Chronos-2 is not natively a deterministic point-forecasting engine; it is a probabilistic foundation model trained via cross-entropy loss to output quantized predictive distributions.[2, 3] By evaluating the model strictly on point forecasts (RMSE/MASE), the manuscript ignores the model's primary architectural strength and ignores a major trend in the IJF literature, which increasingly emphasizes density forecasting and the quantification of macroeconomic uncertainty.[1, 37, 38, 39] The agentic search should ideally be re-configured to optimize a strictly proper scoring rule, such as the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (CRPS) or the scaled Quantile Loss (SQL).[2, 40] If point forecasts must remain the primary focus, the manuscript must be revised to clarify exactly how the point forecast is extracted from Chronos-2's predictive probability distribution (e.g., is it the mean, the median, or the mode of the sampled trajectories?).[4]

**6. Prompt Contamination and the Fallacy of Autonomous "Discovery"**
In Section 5.5, the authors assert that the LLM agent autonomously "discovers" an economically interpretable configuration: "The agent's search trajectory reveals a clear pattern: it builds up a set of economically interpretable covariates one at a time... The four accepted covariates map onto standard macro transmission channels for a small open oil-exporting economy".[1] 

This narrative of autonomous scientific discovery is heavily compromised by the explicit prompt design utilized to govern the agent. In Section 3.2, the authors admit that the natural-language specification (program.md) provided to Claude Sonnet includes explicit domain knowledge, such as: "Norway is an oil-exporting economy; Brent crude and exchange rates are highly relevant".[1] By explicitly instructing the LLM on which variables to prioritize within the prompt, the agent did not autonomously "discover" these macroeconomic transmission channels; it simply acted as a compliant parser, executing the human authors' pre-existing theoretical biases.

This methodology borders on prompt contamination. The claim of autonomous feature discovery must be heavily qualified or entirely rewritten. To genuinely test the capabilities of agentic search in econometrics, the authors must conduct a rigorous ablation study. The experiment must be re-run utilizing an "uninformed" agent—an LLM provided only with anonymized variable tickers (e.g., VAR_1, VAR_2) without any semantic descriptions, economic context, or human hints. Does the uninformed agent still select Brent crude and the policy rate purely through empirical loss reduction? If the uninformed agent fails to find the optimal configuration, the procedure described in this manuscript is not "automated machine learning" [41], but rather "LLM parameter-fitting strictly bounded by human macroeconomic priors." This distinction is vital for the integrity of the research.

### Minor comments

1.  **Chronos-2 Architecture and Quantization Mechanics:** In Section 3.1, the manuscript describes Chronos-2's architecture with extreme brevity.[1] Given the technically proficient audience of the IJF, a more rigorous mathematical description of how Chronos-2 handles scaling and quantization is necessary. Time series values in the Chronos architecture are scaled and quantized into discrete tokens before being fed into the T5 language model backbone.[2, 3] How does this specific quantization protocol handle the massive variance spikes and extreme outliers observed during the 2020 COVID-19 shock? The authors should explicitly discuss whether the quantization bins effectively cap or smooth extreme outliers, which could theoretically explain the zero-shot model's surprising robustness during crises.[40]
2.  **Covariate Dimensionality and Group Attention:** The paper states Chronos-2 natively integrates covariates via a "group attention" mechanism.[3] However, it is vital to mathematically clarify the dimensionality and temporal alignment of these covariates. Are they aligned step-by-step with the target series? Does the model receive the covariates exclusively as past-only series, or as known-future variables? The exact input tensor shape must be defined.
3.  **Literature Review Omissions:** The review of foundation models in Section 2.2 provides a good overview but misses critical architectural nuances. The authors mention TimesFM and MOIRAI but should explicitly note that TimesFM relies on a patch-level tokenization mechanism rather than continuous quantization [4], and MOIRAI utilizes a masked encoder architecture.[42] Furthermore, integrating recent works that evaluate TSFMs explicitly for economic or agricultural forecasting (e.g., Wang & Zhang, 2026, on commodity prices) [5, 11] would significantly strengthen the literature review.
4.  **Forward-Filling and Look-Ahead Bias:** In Equation (1), the manuscript formulates the publication lag constraint.[1] The text states that "quarterly series (house prices, Euro area GDP) are forward-filled." Forward-filling quarterly data into a monthly panel in a pseudo-out-of-sample setting is notoriously dangerous and can inadvertently introduce look-ahead bias if the temporal alignment of the forward-fill occurs at the end of the quarter rather than strictly on the actual, delayed publication date. The authors must rigorously define the exact calendar day mechanics of this alignment process to assure the reader that no future data leaks into the monthly forecast origin.
5.  **Agent Acceptance Rates and Non-Stationarity:** Appendix D notes that out of 50 agentic iterations, only 6 were accepted, yielding a highly inefficient 12% acceptance rate.[1] The manuscript notes that attempts by the LLM to transform covariates (e.g., applying log-differences) consistently worsened the forecasting scores. This is a highly counterintuitive result for macroeconomic data like Brent Crude or global equity indices, where raw levels are typically highly non-stationary and standard econometric theory mandates differencing. The authors must engage with this anomaly and hypothesize *why* the Chronos-2 neural network prefers non-stationary raw levels. Is it because the internal context-window scaling mechanism of the TSFM negates the need for standard unit-root differencing? This is an excellent theoretical discussion point that is currently entirely absent.
6.  **Hyperparameter Search Space Constraints:** Table 1 lists the available fine-tuning learning rates as varying strictly from $10^{-6}$ to $10^{-4}$.[1] LoRA adaptation on large language models and TSFMs frequently requires slightly higher learning rates than full-parameter fine-tuning. The restriction of the upper bound to $10^{-4}$ might mechanically explain why only the highly conservative setting of 100 steps with a $5 \times 10^{-6}$ learning rate was accepted by the agent; the model might have been artificially trapped in a suboptimal local minimum by the search boundaries. The authors should consider expanding the hyperparameter grid substantially.
7.  **Table Formatting and Precision:** In Table 5, which details the subperiod results [1], the use of bold formatting is missing to clearly indicate the best performing model per horizon, making the tables difficult to parse quickly. Furthermore, the RMSE values are reported to three decimal places. Given the inherent volatility of macroeconomic variables, standard errors for these RMSE estimates should be provided in parentheses below the point estimates to allow readers to gauge the precision and variance of the out-of-sample estimates.
8.  **Equation Notation for Stationary Targets:** In the mathematical definition of the random walk baseline, the manuscript states $\hat{y}_{t+h|t}=y_{t}$.[1] This explicitly assumes the target variable is an integrated I(1) process. However, the target variable CPI inflation is already defined as a "12-month rate of change" (Table 6). Forecasting a 12-month rate of change with a pure random walk $\hat{y}_{t+h|t}=y_{t}$ is effectively imposing a random walk with drift on the underlying price level. The authors must clarify if this naive baseline is mathematically appropriate for stationary target variables like the unemployment rate, or if a mean-reverting baseline would be more theoretically sound.
9.  **ARIMA Search Space Restrictions:** The ARIMA order is selected by AIC over a strictly constrained grid of $p \in \{0,1,2,3\}$, $d \in \{0,1\}$, $q \in \{0,1,2\}$.[1] Constraining the autoregressive order to a maximum of 3 for monthly data is severely insufficient. Monthly macroeconomic series frequently exhibit seasonal dynamics at lag 12, or require at least $p=6$ to capture standard half-year business cycles. The arbitrary restriction of $p \le 3$ severely and artificially handicaps the ARIMA baseline. The search space must be expanded to at least $p \le 12$ (matching the AR(p) baseline parameters).
10. **The Dynamics of Exchange Rate Selection:** The NOK/EUR exchange rate is added by the agent in iteration 39.[1] However, Norway is a small open economy heavily dependent on the global oil market, which is strictly priced in USD. Did the LLM agent explicitly evaluate the NOK/USD exchange rate (which is present in the variable panel) and actively reject it in favor of NOK/EUR? If so, this provides a fascinating empirical insight into Norwegian macroeconomic transmission channels (suggesting trade-weighted impacts outweigh pure commodity pricing channels) that merits a dedicated analytical paragraph.
11. **The Omission of Unstructured Textual Data:** The authors themselves have a rich and highly cited history of utilizing unstructured textual data and news-based emotion indexes to accurately forecast Norwegian macroeconomic dynamics (e.g., Thorsrud, 2016; Larsen et al., 2021).[43, 44, 45, 46] Given that foundation models are inherently built upon natural language processing architectures and process text natively, the authors miss a massive intellectual opportunity to discuss why unstructured text features were excluded from the covariate pool. TSFMs theoretically possess the unique ability to seamlessly bridge numerical time-series embeddings and textual embeddings.
12. **Context Window Interpretability:** The LLM agent optimally truncates the lookback context window to exactly 96 months (8 years).[1] The authors briefly suggest that "very old data introduces noise." This empirical finding aligns perfectly with the established literature on time-varying parameters and structural breaks (e.g., Koop and Korobilis, 2013) [1], which argues that downweighting distant observations improves forecast accuracy in changing economies. This theoretical connection between neural network context windows and econometric parameter drift should be explicitly drawn in the discussion section.
13. **Data Panel Transparency and Variable Units:** Table 6 provides the variable catalog.[1] However, the specific units of measurement for the target variables are not explicitly defined in the table. Are Industrial Production and Retail Sales measured in raw index points (e.g., 2015=100) or as month-over-month percentage growth rates? This distinction is critical for interpreting the absolute magnitude of the reported RMSE values (e.g., an RMSE of 2.68 for Industrial Production has vastly different statistical implications if the variable is a bounded index level versus a volatile percentage growth rate).
14. **Agentic Stability and the Git Ratchet Revert:** In the description of the AutoResearch loop (Algorithm 1) [8], it is stated that if a proposal is rejected, the configuration is immediately reverted via a git reset.[8] Does the LLM agent retain an episodic memory of *why* a specific configuration was rejected? LLM agents operating in iterative loops frequently suffer from "looping" or "hallucination" behavior where they propose the exact same failed hyperparameter configuration multiple times. The authors must comment on the cognitive stability of the Claude Sonnet agent over the course of the 50 iterations.
15. **Code, Data, and Reproducibility Availability:** As per the strict submission guidelines of the *International Journal of Forecasting*, empirical studies must provide sufficient descriptions, raw data, and executable computer code to allow for full independent replication.[47] The manuscript vaguely notes that the experiment log is available in a "project repository", but there is no explicit link to a GitHub repository containing the AutoGluon configuration scripts, the Claude API natural language prompts, and the data preparation pipeline. A persistent DOI link to the replication package must be included prior to any publication.

### Questions for the authors

To assist the review process during a potential subsequent revision, please address the following specific technical questions in a separate author response document:

1.  **Agentic Temperature and Stochasticity Settings:** What specific temperature parameter setting was utilized for the Claude Sonnet API calls during the agentic search? Was the LLM agent forced to act deterministically (temperature = 0), or was algorithmic stochasticity intentionally allowed to encourage broader exploration of the search space? If stochasticity was permitted, how mathematically robust is the specific pipeline configuration discovered in Iteration 45 to different random initialization seeds? Have you run multiple identical loops to verify convergence stability?
2.  **Computational Budget and AutoML Efficiency:** The manuscript highlights that the search required 50 discrete iterations.[1] What was the exact wall-clock time and the required computational hardware (e.g., GPU hours) required to execute this entire search? Given that standard automated machine learning (AutoML) algorithms, such as Bayesian Optimization or Tree-structured Parzen Estimators, can efficiently search hundreds of complex hyperparameter configurations per hour at a fraction of the computational cost, what is the precise scientific or computational justification for utilizing a token-heavy LLM agent over traditional AutoML techniques for this specific, highly constrained JSON search space?
3.  **Data Leakage and Pre-training Contamination:** Chronos-2 is pre-trained on massive, heterogeneous public datasets, including the LOTSA corpus.[1, 3] Is it statistically possible that the specific Norwegian macroeconomic data downloaded from Statistics Norway (SSB) up to the year 2024 was inadvertently included in the foundation model's pre-training corpus? If Chronos-2 has already "seen" the post-COVID Norwegian data during its massive pre-training phase, the impressive zero-shot performance documented in this study might not be an example of true zero-shot inference, but rather a manifestation of test-set data contamination. How do you rigorously control for, or quantitatively assess, this critical risk of data leakage?
4.  **Target Variable Normalization and Scaling Bias:** You note in the methodology that "Target variables are never transformed, as forecasts must remain in the original scale for evaluation".[1] However, the Chronos-2 architecture internally applies a robust scaling mechanism to the entire context window prior to tokenization to normalize the inputs.[2] Does the AutoGluon wrapper correctly and symmetrically inverse-transform the probabilistic token outputs back to the original macroeconomic scale without introducing systemic bias, especially for macroeconomic variables characterized by heavy-tailed distributions?
5.  **Per-Variable Performance Decomposition:** In the concluding section, you briefly mention the highly critical detail that "the agent-tuned configuration helps unemployment but hurts retail sales".[1] Could you provide a comprehensive summary table showing the validation and test MASE for *each* of the four target variables independently? Understanding precisely which macroeconomic variables benefit from exogenous covariate inclusion and which suffer from increased dimensionality is crucial for practitioners attempting to deploy these models in real-world scenarios.

### Overall assessment

**Recommendation: Major Revision**

This manuscript tackles a highly ambitious, computationally advanced, and empirically relevant topic. The integration of LLM-guided agentic search algorithms with modern time series foundation models represents the absolute frontier of contemporary forecasting research. The paper is exceptionally well-written, the experimental design of the autonomous research loop is cleverly adapted from Karpathy's framework, and the identification of the "overfitting paradox" under severe macroeconomic structural breaks provides a compelling and highly cautionary narrative that will be of immense interest to the sophisticated readership of the *International Journal of Forecasting*.

However, the empirical execution of the study requires substantial and rigorous reinforcement to meet the journal's exacting standards. The current evaluation protocol artificially favors the foundation model by entirely omitting standard multivariate macroeconomic baselines, specifically Bayesian Vector Autoregressions and Dynamic Factor Models. Furthermore, the complete lack of formal statistical testing (such as the Diebold-Mariano test), the reliance on pseudo-real-time data (rather than true, unrevised historical vintages), and the implementation of a static, heavily handicapped "fit-once" fine-tuning approach critically undermine the reliability and generalizability of the stated conclusions.

If the authors can successfully implement recursive/rolling dynamic fine-tuning, incorporate the requested BVAR and DFM baselines, conduct rigorous tests of equal predictive accuracy, and address the critical real-time data caveats, this paper possesses the distinct potential to become a highly cited, seminal contribution to the emerging literature on foundation models in econometrics. I strongly encourage the authors to undertake these necessary, albeit extensive, revisions and resubmit their work for further consideration.
