# Automated Feature Engineering for Macroeconomic Forecasting using Time Series Foundation Models: A Comprehensive Literature Review

The integration of artificial intelligence into econometric forecasting represents a profound paradigm shift in how macroeconomic intelligence is gathered, processed, and projected. For small open, commodity-exporting economies—such as Norway—the forecasting apparatus must navigate highly volatile external shocks, shifting exchange rate dynamics, and collinear domestic indicators. Traditional macroeconometrics has historically relied on rigid structural or reduced-form models, constrained by degrees of freedom and the necessity for manual feature engineering. However, the emergence of Time Series Foundation Models (TSFMs) and large language model (LLM)-driven agentic search mechanisms offers a transformative pathway.

This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level review of the literature underpinning the intersection of macroeconomic forecasting, time series foundation models, and automated machine learning (AutoML) via agentic search. The review is structured across three core strands, culminating in a synthesis that directly supports a methodological framework utilizing an outer-loop agent to optimize covariate selection, data transformations, and fine-tuning configurations for Chronos-2, evaluated through pseudo-out-of-sample validation on Norwegian macroeconomic data.

## Strand 1: Macroeconomic Forecasting

The pursuit of accurate macroeconomic forecasting has been defined by the persistent tension between the desire to incorporate vast amounts of available data and the statistical limitations imposed by the "curse of dimensionality." The literature has evolved significantly from small-scale models to high-dimensional frameworks, and more recently, to the integration of non-linear machine learning techniques.

### High-Dimensional Data, Factor Models, and Diffusion Indexes
Classical forecasting heavily relied on small-scale Vector Autoregression (VAR) models, which quickly exhausted their degrees of freedom when exposed to dozens of macroeconomic indicators. The seminal breakthrough in handling high-dimensional data arrived with Dynamic Factor Models (DFMs) and diffusion indexes, spearheaded by Stock and Watson.[1, 2, 3, 4] DFMs operate on the premise that the co-movement across hundreds of macroeconomic and financial time series can be synthesized into a small number of latent, unobserved dynamic factors. These factors effectively capture the underlying business cycle, allowing for parsimonious predictive regressions. Parallel to this, Forni, Hallin, Lippi, and Reichlin introduced generalized dynamic factor models utilizing spectral density matrices to extract common components, providing robust forecasting performance particularly for industrial production and consumer prices.[5, 6, 7] The literature underscores that while both models outperform simple autoregressions, their relative superiority varies by the specific target variable and forecasting horizon.[7] Furthermore, dealing with structural instability in these models remains a critical area of research, with Stock and Watson (2009) noting that if instability in factor loadings is sufficiently independent across series, the estimated factors can still be well estimated.[8, 9]

### Mixed-Frequency Data, Publication Lags, and Nowcasting
Real-time macroeconomic forecasting is plagued by the "ragged edge" problem—the reality that economic indicators are published with varying lags and at different frequencies (e.g., daily financial data versus quarterly GDP releases). To resolve this, Mixed-Data Sampling (MIDAS) regressions were developed by Ghysels, Santa-Clara, and Valkanov.[10, 11, 12] MIDAS models utilize distributed lag polynomials (such as Almon or Beta weighting) to align high-frequency predictors directly with low-frequency targets, bypassing the information loss inherent in temporal aggregation.[13, 14] Furthermore, Giannone, Reichlin, and Small successfully bridged DFMs with Kalman filtering to estimate the current state of the economy (nowcasting) amid asynchronous data releases.[3, 15] Bridge equations also remain a staple for translating high-frequency data into quarterly estimates, though Factor-MIDAS combinations have proven highly effective at exploiting timely business cycle indicators without succumbing to parameter proliferation.[14, 16]

### Bayesian Shrinkage and Large Macro Panels
While factor models condense data into latent variables, an alternative approach involves applying regularization to retain the explicit economic variables in a large system. Bańbura, Giannone, and Reichlin demonstrated that Bayesian Vector Autoregressions (BVARs) could be scaled to hundreds of variables by applying stringent prior distributions (such as the Minnesota prior), where the degree of shrinkage increases with the cross-sectional dimension.[17, 18, 19, 20] This mechanism effectively controls overfitting and makes large-scale structural analysis computationally feasible, avoiding the inadmissible estimators generated by flat priors.[18] Koop and Korobilis further advanced this literature by developing time-varying parameter BVARs (TVP-BVARs) incorporating dynamic model averaging, allowing the forecasting apparatus to continuously adapt to structural breaks and shifting volatilities over time.[19, 21, 22]

### Machine Learning for Macro Forecasting
In recent years, machine learning (ML) has permeated macroeconomic forecasting, serving not merely as a black-box alternative, but as a catalyst to capture non-linearities and complex interactions. Goulet Coulombe, Leroux, Stevanovic, and Surprenant conducted an exhaustive decomposition of ML forecasting gains, concluding that non-linearity—particularly executed via Random Forests—is the "true game changer" for macroeconomic prediction.[23, 24, 25] Their analysis revealed that ML non-linearities yield substantial forecasting gains primarily during periods of high macroeconomic uncertainty, financial stress, and housing bubble bursts.[26, 27] Furthermore, Babii, Ghysels, and Striaukas formalized the econometrics of ML for economic forecasting, proving the efficacy of high-dimensional regularized regressions (Lasso, Ridge) on mixed-frequency panel data, while emphasizing the necessity of K-fold time series cross-validation.[28] Tree-based methods and neural networks consistently outperform classical approaches in non-linear, high-dimensional contexts, particularly at long horizons and near turning points.[29]

### Forecasting the Norwegian Economy and Forecast Combinations
Applying these methodologies to a small, open, commodity-exporting economy introduces unique complexities. The Norwegian macroeconomy is heavily exposed to global demand shocks, fluctuations in the petroleum sector, and exchange rate volatility. Norges Bank has historically utilized a combination of dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) models (NEMO) and systems for averaging models (SAM) to generate robust forecasts, recognizing that a weighted average of different model-based forecasts is often more accurate than individual predictions.[30] 

Recent literature has expanded the feature space for forecasting the Norwegian economy. Aastveit, Bjørnland, and Cross analyzed the pass-through of global supply and demand shocks, highlighting how inflation expectations act as severe amplifiers in the Norwegian context.[31, 32] Furthermore, to bypass traditional publication lags, Aastveit et al. successfully deployed models leveraging billions of debit card transactions to nowcast Norwegian household consumption.[33] Crucially, Larsen's (2021) work on the "Components of Uncertainty" utilized Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) on a vast corpus of Norwegian newspaper text to construct high-frequency uncertainty indices. This work established that text-derived indicators of "good" and "bad" uncertainty contain significant predictive power for the business cycle, laying the groundwork for automated feature engineering to evaluate unstructured textual data.[34, 35, 36]

### Evaluation Methodology: Pseudo-Out-Of-Sample and Density Forecasting
The literature demands rigorous out-of-sample evaluation to validate forecasting models. Pseudo-out-of-sample exercises, utilizing expanding or rolling windows, are standard practice to simulate the real-time constraints faced by policymakers.[37, 38] The Diebold-Mariano test remains the foundational statistical method for comparing predictive accuracy between competing models.[26] Increasingly, the literature has shifted from point forecasts to density and probabilistic forecasting, providing decision-makers with a full distribution of risks. Lenza et al. utilized quantile regression forests for macroeconomic forecasting, while Aastveit et al. demonstrated the efficacy of density forecasts utilizing MIDAS models, capturing the evolving distribution of future outcomes.[39, 40, 41]

## Strand 2: Foundation Models for Time Series

The success of Large Language Models (LLMs) in natural language processing has inspired a corresponding revolution in time series analysis. Traditional local models (like ARIMA or standard LSTMs) are constrained by their inductive biases, reliance on single-task training, and their inability to generalize beyond independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) training data.[42] The current frontier is dominated by Time Series Foundation Models (TSFMs), which are pre-trained on heterogeneous, large-scale data corpora to enable robust zero-shot and few-shot inference across diverse domains.

### Architecture, Tokenization, and Amazon's Chronos-2
The adaptation of transformer architectures to numerical time series necessitates novel tokenization and embedding strategies. The literature presents two dominant paradigms: patch-based continuous representations and discrete tokenization. 

Amazon's **Chronos** and its successor **Chronos-2** (Ansari et al., 2024, 2025) treat time series forecasting strictly as a language modeling problem.[43, 44] The architecture scales continuous values by their absolute mean and quantizes them into a fixed number of discrete bins (e.g., 4096 tokens), allowing the use of an off-the-shelf T5 encoder-decoder framework trained via cross-entropy loss.[45, 46] The critical advancement in Chronos-2 is the introduction of a "group attention mechanism." This architecture alternates between time attention layers (aggregating across patches within a single series) and group attention layers, facilitating in-context learning (ICL) by sharing information across arbitrary-sized groups of time series. This enables robust zero-shot handling of multivariate data and complex covariates (both past-only and known future indicators, such as weather or holidays).[43, 47] In empirical tests on the fev-bench and GIFT-Eval benchmarks, Chronos-2 significantly outperforms existing models on tasks involving covariates.[47, 48]

### TimesFM, Lag-Llama, and MOIRAI
Conversely, Google's **TimesFM** (Das et al., 2024) utilizes a decoder-only architecture combined with patch-level tokenization, where contiguous time points (e.g., 32 observations) are processed as a single high-dimensional vector.[49] This approach drastically expands the effective context window. Recently, TimesFM introduced "In-Context Fine-Tuning" (ICFT), allowing the model to adapt to new domains using few-shot examples presented in the prompt without modifying the underlying model weights, mimicking the behavior of LLM prompting.[49]

Other prominent models approach the forecasting problem differently. **Lag-Llama** (Rasul et al., 2023) focuses explicitly on univariate probabilistic forecasting, utilizing a LLaMA-based decoder-only structure that explicitly incorporates lagged values and calendar features as covariates, outputting probabilistic distributions.[50, 51] Salesforce's **MOIRAI** initially pioneered any-variate universal forecasting via masked-encoder techniques. However, with Moirai 2.0 (Liu et al., 2025), the architecture transitioned to a decoder-only backbone utilizing recursive multi-quantile decoding. Notably, the authors explicitly dropped multivariate support and covariate handling due to a lack of high-quality pre-training corpora that feature these properties, citing the need for synthetic data generation to overcome this in the future.[52, 53, 54] 

### LLM Reprogramming and Pre-training Corpora
A distinct sub-field involves "reprogramming" pre-trained textual LLMs for numerical forecasting. Models such as **Time-LLM** (Jin et al., 2024), **PromptCast** (Xue & Salim, 2023), and **UniTime** (Liu et al., 2024) freeze the expansive reasoning capabilities of models like GPT-4 or LLaMA, utilizing linear projectors or domain-specific prompts (Prompt-as-Prefix) to map numerical time series patches into the semantic token space of the language model.[55, 56, 57, 58, 59] While these models leverage powerful reasoning and multi-modal knowledge, they often condense the generative output into a single discriminative forward pass, occasionally sacrificing the deep autoregressive temporal modeling seen in dedicated TSFMs.

The efficacy of TSFMs is inextricably linked to their pre-training data. Models are trained on massive compilations like the LOTSA corpus, generating debate regarding test-set contamination and the "evaluation crisis" mirroring NLP. Aksu et al. (2024) highlight that the multi-purpose use of datasets across training and zero-shot evaluation severely complicates fair benchmarking.[60, 61, 62] To mitigate this, models like Chronos-2 heavily utilize synthetic data generated via Gaussian processes and KernelSynth to enforce diverse structural dynamics without exposing the model directly to specific downstream macroeconomic benchmarks.[63, 64]

### Transfer Learning: Fine-tuning vs. Zero-Shot
The debate between zero-shot inference and fine-tuning remains central to TSFM application. While zero-shot forecasting democratizes access to sophisticated models, fine-tuning on task-specific historical data often yields state-of-the-art results for mission-critical applications.[51, 65, 66] The proposed research specifically interrogates this dynamic, assessing whether agent-guided fine-tuning of Chronos-2 outperforms its highly capable zero-shot baseline within the noisy domain of macroeconomic forecasting.

## Strand 3: Automated Machine Learning and Agentic Search

The trajectory of machine learning optimization has evolved rapidly from manual heuristic tuning to algorithmic search, and currently, toward fully autonomous agentic workflows guided by large language models.

### Traditional AutoML and Feature Engineering
Early iterations of Automated Machine Learning (AutoML) sought to minimize human intervention in hyperparameter optimization (HPO) and model selection. Frameworks like Auto-WEKA, Auto-sklearn (Feurer et al., 2015, 2020), and AutoGluon (Erickson et al., 2020) employ Bayesian optimization, meta-learning, and successive halving (Hyperband) to efficiently navigate the configuration space of classical algorithms.[67, 68, 69] In the context of time series, Automated Feature Engineering (AutoFE) frameworks such as VEST and Dataforge automatically generate rolling windows, lag variables, and Fourier terms, significantly reducing the labor-intensive preprocessing phase.[70, 71, 72, 73] Research demonstrates that combining AutoFE with AutoML yields superior predictive accuracy compared to isolated AutoML applications.[71, 74] Similarly, Neural Architecture Search (NAS) applies strategies like Tree-structured Parzen Estimators to optimize network topologies for volatile financial time series.[75, 76] However, these systems rely on rigidly defined search spaces and predefined optimization heuristics, rendering them incapable of reasoning about novel model architectures or creatively integrating unstructured domain knowledge.

### The Emergence of LLM-Guided Agentic Search
By 2024–2026, the paradigm shifted toward "agentic search," wherein LLMs are utilized not just as predictive engines, but as meta-optimizers capable of reading, reasoning, and autonomously altering experimental code.[77] Sakana AI's introduction of "The AI Scientist" (Lu et al., 2024) and its v2 iteration (Yamada et al., 2025) demonstrated the feasibility of end-to-end scientific discovery.[78, 79, 80] Utilizing a progressive agentic tree-search methodology, The AI Scientist autonomously hypothesizes algorithmic changes, executes the code, analyzes visual outputs via Vision-Language Models (VLMs), and generates peer-reviewed quality manuscripts.[78, 80] The broader literature envisions Agentic Time Series Forecasting (ATSF), conceptualizing forecasting not as a single-pass computation, but as an multi-turn interaction process encompassing perception, planning, action, and reflection.[81]

### Karpathy’s Autoresearch Framework
A highly focused, robust execution of this agentic concept is Andrej Karpathy's "autoresearch" framework, released in March 2026.[82, 83, 84] Designed to automate neural architecture search and optimization overnight, the framework enforces a strict "ratchet loop" governed by three core files:
1.  `prepare.py`: An immutable script handling deterministic data ingestion, tokenization, and strict evaluation protocols.[82]
2.  `train.py`: A fully mutable sandbox where the LLM agent is permitted to rewrite model architectures, adjust learning rates, or implement novel optimizers (e.g., Muon).[82]
3.  `program.md`: The research director's constitutional document, providing the context, constraints, and optimization goals to the agent.[82, 85]

The agent continuously proposes a code modification, runs a fixed-budget (e.g., 5-minute) training cycle, and evaluates the model against a vocabulary-independent metric like `val_bpb` (validation bits per byte). If the metric improves, the `git commit` is retained; if not, the system autonomously reverts the state.[82, 86, 87] This framework brilliantly isolates the search environment, ensuring fair cross-experiment comparability and perfect reproducibility—addressing the severe "claim-governance" and validation issues that plague opaque AI experimentation.[88, 89] The framework has seen rapid adoption, with forks demonstrating its efficacy on varied hardware (Apple Silicon MLX, Windows RTX) and in disciplines ranging from economics to citizen-science phenotypic extraction.[82, 90]

### Application to Macroeconomic Features
By integrating agentic search with automated feature engineering, researchers can transcend the limits of manual indicator selection. In the context of the Norwegian economy, the agent can be directed to search across diverse lag structures, non-linear transformations, and high-dimensional exogenous inputs—including the text-derived newspaper uncertainty topics constructed by Larsen (2021) [34]—to empirically identify which features genuinely minimize out-of-sample forecast errors without succumbing to spurious correlations or data leakage.[91, 92] Explainable AI methodologies, such as SHAP (Shapley Additive exPlanations), are also increasingly integrated into these automated pipelines to ensure transparent identification of the underlying economic drivers behind the forecasts.[93]

### Key Gaps Addressed by the Proposed Research
While agentic frameworks have demonstrated immense capability in optimizing neural network pre-training loops and general data engineering, they have not been systematically applied to the rigorous, pseudo-out-of-sample environments demanded by macroeconomic forecasting. The literature lacks a unified study that bridges agentic search (the outer loop) with the specific task of selecting complex economic covariates and fine-tuning advanced TSFMs like Chronos-2.

## Synthesis and Intersection: Gaps Addressed by the Proposed Framework

The trajectory of the surveyed literature points toward an inevitable convergence: the rigidity of traditional econometric factor models (Strand 1) is being disrupted by the zero-shot capabilities of Time Series Foundation Models (Strand 2), which in turn require sophisticated, automated orchestration to handle high-dimensional, noisy data effectively (Strand 3). 

The proposed research occupies the nexus of these three strands by executing a three-way decomposition of forecast gains in the context of a small, open commodity-exporting economy. Recent papers increasingly combine these domains; for instance, researchers have begun integrating traditional statistical forecasting models with machine learning algorithms using AutoML frameworks to analyze complex expenditures [94], while others leverage LLMs specifically to extract sentiment features from unstructured data for macroeconomic models.[95]

First, by benchmarking Chronos-2 against classical macro models (e.g., BVARs, DFMs), the research isolates the value of treating macroeconomic series as tokenized language constructs. This directly addresses the gap in TSFM literature regarding their comparative efficacy against established baselines on highly collinear, structural-break-prone economic data evaluated through rigorous Diebold-Mariano testing.

Second, by explicitly contrasting zero-shot performance with fine-tuned configurations, the study quantifies the exact marginal benefit of domain-specific adaptation for foundation models in macroeconomics, testing whether the "in-context learning" of Chronos-2 is sufficient or if weight updates are required for volatile indicators.

Finally, and most crucially, the deployment of an agentic search procedure—inspired by Karpathy's autoresearch—to govern the feature engineering and model tuning pipeline directly addresses the limitations of manual covariate selection. By allowing the agent to autonomously evaluate unconventional features—such as the LDA-derived textual uncertainty metrics developed in Larsen (2021)—against a strict rolling pseudo-out-of-sample loss function, the research determines whether AI agents can empirically uncover predictive macroeconomic relationships that human researchers might overlook. This intersection not only pushes the boundary of how foundation models are utilized in economics but also establishes a reproducible, claim-governed framework for the future of automated econometric research.
