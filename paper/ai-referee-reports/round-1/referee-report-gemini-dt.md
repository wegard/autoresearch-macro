### Summary

The manuscript investigates the application of an LLM-guided "agentic search" procedure to optimize a time series foundation model (Chronos-2) for macroeconomic forecasting in a small open economy (Norway). The authors employ a rigorous pseudo-real-time evaluation protocol that respects publication lags to forecast four key macroeconomic variables over a 2006–2025 expanding window. The LLM agent (Claude Sonnet) is tasked with selecting covariates, transformations, context lengths, and fine-tuning hyperparameters based on a rolling validation window.

The paper documents a fascinating and highly relevant empirical finding: while the agent successfully identifies an economically interpretable pipeline configuration during the validation era (2006–2015)—reducing MASE by 6.8% compared to the zero-shot baseline—this tuned pipeline severely overfits out of sample. During the test era (2016–2025), which encompasses the COVID-19 pandemic and the post-2022 inflation surge, the agent-tuned model fails to generalize. In contrast, the zero-shot Chronos-2 foundation model demonstrates remarkable robustness to these structural breaks, outperforming both the tuned model and classical univariate baselines. 

This paper sits at the frontier of forecasting methodology, blending foundation models and automated machine learning (AutoML) with rigorous macroeconomic evaluation. The central finding—that pre-trained zero-shot robustness can dominate explicitly tuned covariate pipelines during regime shifts—is practically valuable and highly relevant to the readership of the *International Journal of Forecasting*. However, the manuscript is explicitly marked as "preliminary and incomplete," and it suffers from critical methodological flaws regarding error metric aggregation, evaluation protocols, and baseline selection that must be addressed before it can be considered for publication.

### Major comments

1. **Invalid aggregation of forecast errors:** In Tables 2, 4, and 5, you report the "average RMSE across targets." According to Section 3.3, target variables are never transformed and remain in their original scale. Averaging raw RMSE across variables with fundamentally different units, scales, and variances (e.g., an unemployment rate of ~3–4% versus a retail sales volume index) is mathematically invalid. The aggregated RMSE will be entirely dominated by the variables with the largest nominal scale, masking the performance on the smaller-scale rates. You must report the forecast errors for each target variable individually (as promised in the currently empty Appendix C). If an aggregate metric is necessary for summary tables, you must use a scale-independent metric like MASE (which you correctly use for the agent's search metric).
2. **Missing multivariate baselines:** A core premise of the paper is evaluating whether the agent can discover a useful high-dimensional representation for the foundation model. However, you evaluate this multivariate agent-tuned pipeline exclusively against *univariate* classical baselines (Random Walk, Seasonal Naive, AR, ARIMA, ETS). To legitimately claim that the agent has discovered a useful multivariate representation, the baseline set must include at least one standard data-rich macroeconomic model, such as a Bayesian VAR (BVAR) or a Dynamic Factor Model (DFM) a la Stock and Watson. You correctly acknowledge this omission in your `[TODO]` in Section 6; executing this is mandatory for an IJF submission.
3. **Flawed fine-tuning protocol ("Fit-Once"):** In Section 3.1, you state that *"fine-tuning is performed once on the data available at the first forecast origin, and the adapted model is reused for all subsequent origins."* In an expanding window design spanning nearly two decades, freezing the fine-tuned weights in January 2006 is highly problematic. By 2025, the fine-tuned model is utilizing network parameters adapted to a 20-year-old economic environment. This computational shortcut severely confounds your conclusion about the fragility of the agent's covariate selection; the catastrophic out-of-sample failure of the tuned model may simply be due to stale model weights, especially since the classical baselines are re-estimated at every origin. To make a fair comparison, the foundation model should be periodically re-tuned (e.g., annually) to reflect the expanding information set.
4. **Lack of statistical significance testing:** The paper concludes that certain models "beat" others based entirely on point differences in average loss metrics. In the macroeconomic forecasting literature, it is standard practice to test whether these predictive gains are statistically distinguishable from noise. You mention the Diebold-Mariano (2002) test in your literature review but fail to implement it. You must include formal tests of equal predictive accuracy (e.g., Diebold-Mariano or the Model Confidence Set procedure) to substantiate your out-of-sample performance claims, particularly given the short and highly volatile subperiods (e.g., the 24-month COVID-19 era).
5. **Incomplete manuscript elements:** The submitted draft contains several literal `[TODO]` tags (e.g., in the abstract, author affiliations, Section 5.4, Section 6, and Appendix C). The promised variable-specific breakdowns in Appendix C are missing. A journal submission must be a complete, finalized manuscript.

### Minor comments

1. **Metric inconsistency:** The search trajectory (Table 3) is presented using MASE, but the out-of-sample results (Tables 4 and 5) report average RMSE. Please standardize the reporting metric throughout the paper (preferably MASE) so readers can track the exact magnitude of performance degradation from the validation to the test era.
2. **Acceptance logic in agent search:** In Table 3, Iteration 39 achieves a MASE of 1.8129. Iteration 45 (adding LoRA fine-tuning) also achieves a MASE of 1.8129. Algorithm 1 clearly states `if full score < current best then Accept`. Since the score did not strictly improve, why was this configuration accepted by the agent? Please clarify the tie-breaking logic.
3. **Data availability anomaly:** In Table 6, the unemployment rate (LFS) is listed as being available from `2006-01`. However, the validation era also begins in `2006-01`. Given that the agent selects a context length of 96 months, how does the foundation model (and the autoregressive baselines) handle the missing 95 months of historical context at the very beginning of the validation window?
4. **Typographical error in learning rate:** In Table 3 (Iteration 45) and Section 5.2, the learning rate is written as $5\times10^{-6^{2}}$. This appears to be a LaTeX typo and should likely be $5\times10^{-6}$.
5. **Data revisions versus pseudo-real-time:** You rigorously model publication lags (Eq 1), which is excellent. However, using latest-vintage data removes the noise of data revisions. Macroeconomic variables like Industrial Production and Retail Sales are often heavily revised. A brief sentence acknowledging how the lack of true real-time data vintages might impact the findings (e.g., potentially flattering the models' predictive accuracy) would strengthen your discussion.

### Questions for the authors

1. **Ablation of Fine-Tuning vs. Covariates:** If the 4 covariates selected by the agent (oil prices, policy rate, US CPI, NOK/EUR) were fed into the zero-shot Chronos-2 model *without* the static LoRA fine-tuning, does the out-of-sample performance still degrade so severely? Conducting this ablation would definitively isolate whether the regime change invalidated the covariate relationships themselves, or if the static fine-tuning weights were the primary culprit.
2. **Agentic Adaptability:** Could the "overfitting paradox" be mitigated by utilizing an expanding-window search—i.e., allowing the LLM agent to re-evaluate and dynamically adjust the pipeline covariates (e.g., every 2–3 years) as new data arrives in the test era, rather than permanently freezing the configuration in 2015? 
3. **Computational Cost:** What is the approximate computational cost (in terms of GPU hours and API token expenditure) required to run the 50-iteration agentic search? Providing this context would greatly help forecasting practitioners judge the practical feasibility of adopting this AutoML workflow.

### Overall assessment

**Recommendation: Major Revision**

This paper tackles a highly relevant and timely topic for the *International Journal of Forecasting*. The application of LLM-guided autoresearch to macroeconomic forecasting is genuinely novel, and the central finding—that pre-trained zero-shot robustness can dominate agent-discovered covariate pipelines during structural breaks—is a highly valuable cautionary tale for the forecasting community. 

However, the manuscript currently suffers from critical methodological flaws that preclude publication in its current state. Most urgently, averaging unscaled RMSE across variables of vastly different magnitudes mathematically invalidates the core results tables. Furthermore, the absence of any multivariate macroeconomic baselines (e.g., BVAR or DFM) makes it impossible to judge whether Chronos-2 is actually extracting useful signals from the chosen covariates compared to standard econometric methods. Finally, the unrealistic "fit-once" fine-tuning approach biases the out-of-sample results, and the lack of statistical significance testing (Diebold-Mariano) must be rectified.

I strongly encourage the authors to undertake a major revision. If the forecast errors are correctly aggregated (via per-variable tables and scale-independent metrics like MASE), a BVAR/DFM baseline is introduced, the fine-tuning protocol is adjusted (or suitably ablated), and the `TODO` placeholders are resolved, this paper has the potential to make an excellent and highly cited contribution to the literature on time series foundation models.
